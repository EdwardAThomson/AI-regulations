# Coordinated Plan on Artificial Intelligence — 2021 Review

**Document:** Coordinated Plan on Artificial Intelligence — 2021 Review
**Reference:** COM(2021) 205 final, Annex to "Fostering a European approach to Artificial Intelligence"
**Published:** 21 April 2021 (same day as the Commission's AI Act proposal COM(2021) 206)
**Issuing body:** European Commission (Directorate-General for Communications Networks, Content and Technology — DG CONNECT), with Member States
**Length:** 66 pages (with two appendices: Timeline of Key Actions; Analysis of National Strategies and Investments in AI)
**Legal status:** Non-binding joint planning document; policy communication and coordination framework
**Predecessor:** 2018 Coordinated Plan on AI (COM(2018) 795 final); accompanied by 2018 European Commission Communication on AI for Europe (COM(2018) 237 final) and the 2020 White Paper on AI (COM(2020) 65 final)

---

## Summary

The 2021 review of the 2018 Coordinated Plan is the European Commission's strategic and investment blueprint for making the EU a global leader in trustworthy, human-centric AI, published in tandem with the first draft of the AI Act. It organises joint actions between the Commission and Member States around three verbs — **Accelerate** (investments), **Act** (implement national strategies), **Align** (remove fragmentation). It sets a target of **EUR 20 billion per year** in combined public-private AI investment across the decade, with EU-level contributions of at least EUR 1 billion per year from Horizon Europe (HE) and Digital Europe (DEP), alongside the Recovery and Resilience Facility (RRF) and InvestEU. The document structures its 14 joint action areas into **four pillars**: (I) set enabling conditions for AI development and uptake (governance, data, computing capacity); (II) make the EU the place where excellence thrives from the lab to the market (stakeholder partnerships, research capacities, TEFs, EDIHs, innovation funding); (III) ensure that AI works for people (skills, trust framework, international promotion); (IV) build strategic leadership in high-impact sectors (climate/environment, health, robotics, public sector, law enforcement/migration/asylum, mobility, agriculture). Appendix 2 surveys national AI strategies (19 Member States plus Norway had adopted strategies by April 2021).

## Key Points

### The Three Strategic Imperatives

1. **Accelerate** — leverage EU funding (DEP, HE, RRF) to crowd in private investment; target EUR 20 billion per year total public + private
2. **Act** — move from intention to action via concrete, timeline-bound, funded joint actions
3. **Align** — reduce fragmentation between EU and national actions and across funding instruments; cohere with European Green Deal, COVID-19 recovery measures, and the 2020 White Paper on AI

### Investment Framework

- **Horizon Europe + Digital Europe**: minimum EUR 1 billion per year from the EU over 2021-2027
- **RRF** ("Recovery and Resilience Facility"): EUR 672.5 billion loans and grants, with 20% ("digital target") ≈ EUR 134 billion potentially available for digital (including AI) investments over the RRF lifecycle
- **InvestEU 2021-2027**: continues the AI/Blockchain Investment pilot programme
- **EIC** (European Innovation Council): EUR 1 billion+ in grants and equity in 2021 for deep-tech start-ups and SMEs
- **EIT** (European Institute of Innovation and Technology): EUR 22 million+ to AI activities in 2019-2020; supports 120 AI start-ups
- Target: increase combined public + private AI investment to **EUR 20 billion per year over this decade** (from 2021 baseline)

### Pillar I — Set Enabling Conditions (Horizontal Actions 1-3)

**1. Acquire, pool and share policy insights**
- 19 Member States + Norway had national AI strategies by April 2021 (see Appendix 2)
- HLEG replaced by three expert groups: HLEG on AI, HLEG on Digital Transformation of EU Labour Markets, Expert Group on Liability and New Technologies
- AI Watch — JRC-hosted observatory providing national AI strategy analysis, AI uptake monitoring
- Annual AI Assembly for the AI Alliance

**2. Tap into the potential of data**
- Data Act proposal (subsequently adopted 2023)
- Data Governance Act (adopted 2022)
- Open Data Directive; Public sector high-value datasets
- Nine sectoral Common European Data Spaces: health, industrial/manufacturing, agriculture, energy, mobility, finance, public administration, skills, Green Deal, and a European cloud federation
- Alignment with GDPR; data altruism

**3. Foster critical computing capacity**
- European High-Performance Computing Joint Undertaking (EuroHPC JU) — pre-exascale and exascale supercomputers
- Industrial Alliance on Microelectronics
- Key Digital Technologies Joint Undertaking (KDT JU) — chips for AI
- EuroHPC → LUMI (Finland), Leonardo (Italy), MareNostrum5 (Spain) among deployed systems

### Pillar II — Excellence from Lab to Market (Horizontal Actions 4-7)

**4. Stakeholder collaboration**
- Co-programmed European Partnership on AI, Data and Robotics (started Q2 2021) — EUR 2.6 billion total funding envelope
- Expert groups: AI HLEG, ENISA Ad Hoc Expert Group on AI cybersecurity

**5. Build and mobilise research capacities**
- **AI Lighthouse for Europe** — Networks of AI excellence centres under Horizon Europe
- Networks: ELLIS, CLAIRE, EurAI
- Marie Skłodowska-Curie Actions — doctoral and postdoctoral networks focused on AI

**6. AI-on-demand platform + TEFs + EDIHs**
- **TEFs (Testing and Experimentation Facilities)** — four sectoral TEFs proposed: health, manufacturing, agri-food, smart cities/communities; world-class facilities from 2022 onward
- **EDIHs (European Digital Innovation Hubs)** — ~200 hubs across EU; support SMEs and public administrations adopt AI and other digital technologies
- **AI-on-demand platform** — central European AI toolbox/marketplace for algorithms, datasets, tools

**7. Fund and scale innovative AI ideas**
- EIC Pathfinder Challenge 2021 focused on "AI awareness research"
- EIC Accelerator Challenge on strategic digital/health technologies, including medical AI
- Women TechEU initiative for deep-tech start-ups founded by women
- Startup Europe + Innovation Radar

### Pillar III — AI Works for People (Horizontal Actions 8-10)

**8. Nurture talent and improve skills supply**
- Digital Education Action Plan 2021-2027
- EU Code Week (14M+ participants by end 2020; ~50% girls)
- Four university networks awarded EUR 6.5M in December 2020 for EU-wide master's programmes in AI (human-centric AI, AI for public administration, AI for healthcare) with required AI ethics courses
- Gender equality target: 45% female participation in EU-funded AI training activities
- 2022+ Horizon Europe: gender equality plan required for research organisations

**9. Develop a policy framework to ensure trust in AI systems**
- **This is where the review announces the forthcoming AI Act.** Commission to propose in 2021: legislative action on horizontal framework for AI (COM(2021) 206 published same day)
- Commission to propose in 2022: adaptations to liability framework (ultimately withdrawn in 2025)
- Commission to propose in 2021+: revisions to sectoral safety legislation (Machinery Regulation, General Product Safety Regulation, Radio-Equipment Directive)
- EU Cybersecurity Strategy for the Digital Decade (adopted 16 December 2020)
- Intellectual Property Action Plan
- Regulatory sandboxes — wide stakeholder support; already established in several Member States
- **ALTAI (Assessment List for Trustworthy AI)** — developed by HLEG; non-binding self-assessment tool

**10. Promote the EU vision on sustainable and trustworthy AI in the world**
- **Global Partnership on AI (GPAI)** — EU founding member; working groups on data governance, responsible AI, future of work, commercialisation/innovation
- **OECD AI Principles** — EU contributed via ONE AI expert group
- Bilateral AI dialogues: Canada, Japan (first EU-Japan AI committee meeting November 2020), India (joint task force), Australia, Singapore
- **US dialogue**: EU-US Information Society Dialogue; work toward an AI Agreement with the US; later evolved into the Trade and Technology Council (TTC)
- WIPO (AI and IP consultation)
- Dual-Use Regulation revision — finalised April 2021
- UN involvement through various tracks

### Pillar IV — Strategic Leadership in High-Impact Sectors (Actions 11-17)

**11. Climate and environment**
- EU climate targets: 55% GHG reduction by 2030; climate neutrality by 2050
- March 2021: 24 Member States + Norway + Iceland declaration on green digital technologies
- Four AI channels to Green Deal: circular economy, energy system optimisation, decarbonisation, enabling entirely new solutions
- **Destination Earth initiative** — high-quality AI-supported digital simulation of the planet; first phase complete by end 2023
- Research on "frugal AI" (less data-intensive, energy-efficient models)
- Key Performance Indicators for environmental impact of AI

**12. Health**
- **European Health Data Space (EHDS)** — proposed Q4 2021 (ultimately Regulation 2025/327)
- EU Beating Cancer Plan — common health-image database
- "1+ million genomes" initiative
- Digital twins in health
- Horizon Europe projects on trustworthy AI in healthcare
- Pharmaceutical Strategy for Europe (November 2020) — evaluation of general pharmaceutical legislation with AI elements

**13. Robotics**
- AI, Data and Robotics Partnership under Horizon Europe
- Specialised TEFs for AI-powered robots
- Robotics policy observatory under consideration

**14. Public sector**
- **Adopt AI programme** — to support AI procurement
- Local Digital Twins — AI-powered city simulation
- Public procurement data space
- AI algorithm registries (mentioned positively — subsequently adopted by Amsterdam, Helsinki, others)
- **Fair AI MIM (Minimum Interoperability Mechanism)** — reference to OASC framework
- eTranslation — 6,600 civil servants across Member States using the AI-powered portal by end of 2020

**15. Law enforcement, migration, asylum**
- Common European Security Data Space for law enforcement (call Q1 2022)
- Europol strengthened mandate (2020 proposal)
- EU counter-terrorism agenda (December 2020)
- EU Strategy to Tackle Organised Crime (April 2021)
- EU innovation hub for internal security (2020)
- Migration forecasting system pilot
- **Important caveat**: "law enforcement, migration and asylum AI applications are never used as a self-standing 'decision-maker'. AI systems are used to assist... but a human always takes the final decision."

**16. Mobility**
- Sustainable and Smart Mobility Strategy (December 2020)
- AI roadmap on mobility (2021)
- Common European Mobility Data Space
- Aviation: EASA AI Roadmap (February 2020); FLY AI Action Plan (March 2020); Data4Safety project
- Rail: Shift2Rail Joint Undertaking
- Inland waterways: River Information Services / COMEX
- Road: Cooperative, Connected and Automated Mobility (CCAM) Platform; ethics of CAVs (Connected and Automated Vehicles) report
- Implementing acts for automated and fully automated vehicles (applicable from 6 July 2022)
- Access to in-vehicle data rules

**17. Agriculture**
- 2019 Declaration of Cooperation on smart and sustainable agriculture — 25 European countries
- Farm-to-Fork Strategy (May 2020)
- Common European Agriculture Data Space (2023-2024)
- Co-programmed European Partnership on Agriculture of Data
- TEFs and EDIHs for agri-food

### Governance and Implementation

- **AI Alliance + annual AI Assembly** — public-private dialogue
- **Member States' Group on AI and Digitisation of European Industry (DEI)** — formal coordination body
- **Commission + Member States joint action** — monitoring via regular reporting; Commission facilitates, Member States implement

### Appendix 2 — National Strategies (as of April 2021)

**19 Member States + Norway** had adopted national AI strategies. Selected examples:

- **Germany**: 2020 update of AI strategy, 87 measures by 2022; EUR 5 billion committed by 2025 (EUR 2 billion boost in COVID-19 recovery package)
- **France**: Comprehensive approach across sectors
- **Italy**: Sector-specific focus
- **Spain**: 30 measures across six fields; includes Green AI programme; public-private venture capital
- **Estonia**: Comprehensive overview with objectives, deadlines, budget estimations
- **Finland**: Updated 2020 → "AI 4.0" programme
- **Bulgaria**: AI strategy anchored in 2020-2030 Digital Transformation strategy
- **Belgium**: Combination of three regional strategies + federal level
- **Malta, Slovakia**: Horizontal approach — no specific priority sectors
- **Cyprus (maritime), Germany (weather forecasting), Italy (art/culture), Portugal (biodiversity), Latvia (justice), Spain (fashion)**: niche sector priorities
- **Lithuania (energy), Netherlands (water)**: single-sector focus

Most common priority sectors across national strategies: manufacturing, healthcare, agriculture, public administration, transportation, logistics, education, energy.

### Appendix 1 — Timeline of Key Actions (40 numbered actions across four pillars)

Notable timings:
- Action 17: Propose legislative action on horizontal framework on trustworthy AI — **Q2 2021** (delivered: 21 April 2021 as COM(2021) 206, the AI Act proposal)
- Action 8: Establish co-programmed European Partnership on AI, Data and Robotics — Q2 2021
- Action 11: Launch calls for TEFs under DEP — 2021-2022
- Action 12: Set up the network of EDIHs — 2021-2023
- Action 14: Develop AI and Data usage ethical guidelines under Digital Education Action Plan — 2022-2023
- Action 23: Develop roadmap for common European Green Deal data space — Q2 2021
- Action 27: Set up a European Health Data Space — 2021+ (legislative proposal Q4 2021)
- Action 34: Launch deployment and scale-up of AI-powered Urban Digital Twins — Q4 2022

## Observations

### Clarity

The document is structured around three verbs (Accelerate, Act, Align) and four pillars (enabling conditions, excellence, people, sectors), with consistent chapter structure: "Reasons for joint actions" → "Overview of actions taken" → "Outlook" → Commission commitments + Member State encouragements.

Clarity strengths:
- The three verbs are a memorable framing that appears at the opening and in the conclusion
- Each of the 17 chapters follows a consistent internal structure, making it easy to navigate
- The Appendix 1 timeline summarises 40 concrete actions with years marked, providing at-a-glance planning visibility
- Appendix 2 gives a useful, if selective, comparison of Member State approaches

Clarity weaknesses:
- The document is a **political investment plan** — not a regulatory text. Obligations are soft, expressed as "the Commission will..." or "Member States are encouraged to..."
- The breadth (14 joint actions, 40 timeline items, cross-references to ~15 other Commission Communications) makes the document feel overwhelming
- Very few numerical targets (only EUR 1 billion/year EU and EUR 20 billion/year overall are headline numbers)

### Measurability

Measurable elements are limited and tied to funding rather than outcomes:
- EUR 1 billion/year EU from DEP + HE
- EUR 20 billion/year total public + private by end of decade
- RRF ~20% digital target (~EUR 134 billion potentially)
- EIC EUR 1 billion in 2021
- EIT EUR 22 million 2019-2020
- Master's programme grants EUR 6.5 million

Non-measurable or loosely measured:
- "Trustworthy AI" across the document lacks an operational metric
- "Reduce fragmentation" — no baseline and no target
- Impact on business adoption (referenced: global number of AI-using businesses tripled over previous year per Gartner)
- Sector-specific targets exist (55% GHG emissions reduction by 2030 for climate) but are borrowed from other regulatory frameworks

### Gaps

**No binding obligations.** The Plan is political and investment-focused. The binding counterpart — the AI Act — was published on the same day (COM(2021) 206) but is a separate instrument.

**Limited Member State-level accountability.** The document identifies joint actions but lacks mechanisms to compel Member State compliance or funding.

**GPAI not mentioned.** Published in April 2021, pre-ChatGPT (November 2022) and pre-GPAI-emergence as a policy concept. The Plan references "foundation models" only implicitly through NLP advances (GPT-3 reference). Foundation model compliance architecture — now central to the AI Act — is absent.

**No post-2021 review cadence.** The 2018 Plan was reviewed in 2021 but the document does not commit to a regular review cycle. As of early 2026 (now five years later), there has been no comprehensive subsequent review; the Commission's focus has shifted to implementing the AI Act.

**Intellectual property and copyright.** Only briefly mentioned. The AI Act, GDPR, and Directive (EU) 2019/790 on copyright address some aspects, but generative AI copyright (training data, output attribution) was underdeveloped in the Plan and remains contested as of 2026.

**Workforce transition.** Skills are covered but labour-market disruption — job loss, re-skilling at scale, role changes — is treated as a skills-gap problem rather than a broader economic transition.

**AI for military purposes.** Explicitly out of scope: "the 2021 review of the Coordinated Plan does not address the development and use of AI for military purposes." This exclusion is consistent with the AI Act's exemption (Article 2) but leaves a significant strategic technology area unaddressed in EU civilian policy.

**Start-up scaling and EU tech sovereignty.** The Plan mentions start-up scaling (EIC, EIT, InvestEU, Startup Europe) but does not address the deep-tech investment gap between EU and US/China, or the brain-drain of AI talent to the US.

### Feasibility

The Plan is feasible **as a planning document** — many of its announced actions have been implemented (AI Act proposed same day; EDIHs network established; TEFs funded; European Health Data Space regulation adopted 2025; GPAI established; AI, Data and Robotics Partnership active).

Key feasibility questions that the Plan did not fully resolve:
- Absorption capacity of Member States — smaller states struggle to match EUR funding with national contribution
- Coordinated procurement — Adopt AI programme launched but cross-border public procurement for AI remains rare
- Standards — Commission requested European Standardisation Organisations (CEN-CENELEC) to develop AI-related standards; technical standards for AI Act compliance remain in development as of early 2026
- Investment target (EUR 20 billion/year) — limited evidence of sustained achievement; EU AI investment lags US and China

### Internal Consistency

The Plan is internally consistent: the three verbs frame the four pillars; the pillars organise the 17 chapters; the chapters outline specific actions; the timeline in Appendix 1 summarises action years. Cross-references to HE, DEP, RRF, InvestEU, the AI Act proposal, the White Paper, and the GDPR are consistent.

One tension: Pillar III (trust) references the AI Act proposal, which is binding and regulatory, whereas the Plan itself is investment-focused and voluntary. The Plan's "soft power" (funding incentives, coordination) complements the AI Act's "hard power" (binding obligations) but the interaction is not fully mapped.

### Coverage

Broad coverage: 14 joint action areas across horizontal and sectoral dimensions. Key omissions:
- Military AI (explicitly excluded)
- Export control/trade (Dual-Use Regulation is referenced but not a plan focus)
- Intellectual property (brief)
- Labour-market disruption (treated as skills problem)
- Antitrust/competition (referenced briefly in Footnote 13)
- Data-centre infrastructure (referenced implicitly via HPC)

Sectoral coverage is extensive (climate, health, robotics, public sector, law enforcement, migration, asylum, mobility, agriculture) but some sectors are notably absent or underweight: finance (no dedicated chapter despite its centrality in AI adoption), media/entertainment, education (only as a skills topic), legal services, cybersecurity (embedded in Pillar III rather than own chapter).

### International Alignment

The Plan positions the EU as a global leader advocating trustworthy AI. International references:
- OECD AI Principles (May 2019, referenced as aligned vision)
- GPAI (EU founding member, founded July 2020)
- UNESCO (draft Recommendation on the Ethics of AI)
- G7 / G20 AI dialogue
- Council of Europe AI work (later became the 2024 Framework Convention on AI)
- ITU's AI for Good Global Summit

For UK firms, the Plan is historically notable because it is the first EU strategic document post-Brexit. The UK is not a participant; references to "Member States" exclude the UK. The EU-UK coordination framework on AI is not covered.

For US dialogue, the Plan references future "AI Agreement with the US" — this was subsequently subsumed into the Trade and Technology Council (TTC), which was paused in 2024-2025.

### Relationship to EU AI Regulatory Stack

The Plan complements rather than duplicates regulatory instruments. Its function:

- **Investment layer**: what the EU will fund (DEP, HE, RRF, InvestEU)
- **Coordination layer**: how Member States and Commission work together
- **Strategic vision layer**: the "why" — human-centric, sustainable, inclusive, trustworthy AI

Regulatory instruments (AI Act, GDPR, Data Act, Data Governance Act, DSA, DMA, Cyber Resilience Act, European Health Data Space Regulation) are the "what" — the binding rules. The Plan references regulatory instruments but does not replicate them.

## Overall Assessment

The 2021 Review of the Coordinated Plan is the EU's **strategic investment and coordination framework for AI**, published as the non-binding companion to the binding AI Act proposal. It established the financing envelope (EUR 1 billion/year EU; EUR 20 billion/year combined), the delivery infrastructure (TEFs, EDIHs, AI-on-demand platform, partnerships), and the sectoral priorities (climate, health, robotics, public sector, law enforcement, mobility, agriculture).

For UK-based firms providing AI services in the EU, the Plan is historically important for four reasons:

1. **It explains the EU's AI investment priorities.** Funding calls under Horizon Europe and Digital Europe over 2021-2027 broadly track the Plan's structure. UK firms eligible to participate (associated country status for Horizon Europe since 2024) can align proposals with the Plan's 17 priority areas.

2. **It signalled the AI Act's arrival.** Published the same day as COM(2021) 206, the Plan is the political context for understanding the AI Act. Chapter 9 specifically previews the AI Act's content (risk-based, high-risk obligations, conformity assessments, governance mechanism).

3. **It maps the EU's AI delivery infrastructure.** TEFs, EDIHs, AI-on-demand platform, AI, Data and Robotics Partnership — these institutions are the practical access points for AI innovation in Europe. UK firms seeking EU partners, pilots, or regulatory sandboxes typically interact with these structures.

4. **It shows what EU AI does NOT cover.** Military AI is explicitly excluded. Financial services AI is not a priority sector. Copyright for AI-trained models is underdeveloped. UK firms in these areas cannot rely on the Plan for EU-level coordination.

The Plan's greatest strength is **breadth and ambition** — 14 joint action areas, 40 timeline items, EUR 20 billion/year target. Its greatest weakness is **soft enforcement** — Member State encouragement without compulsion, and absence of a subsequent comprehensive review after 2021.

From a 2026 perspective:
- **Delivered**: AI Act (2024); EHDS Regulation (2025); AI, Data and Robotics Partnership (2021); EDIH network; initial TEFs; GPAI Code of Practice (July 2025); Serious Incident Reporting (Sept 2025); Transparency Code first draft (Dec 2025)
- **Delayed or withdrawn**: AI Liability Directive (withdrawn 2025); uneven Member State implementation of national strategies
- **Evolved beyond the Plan**: GPAI emerged as a central concern post-ChatGPT; foundation model regulation became a primary focus; Apply AI Strategy (2025) and AI Factories initiative (2024-2025) have largely replaced the Coordinated Plan as the operational investment framework
- **Current status**: The Coordinated Plan remains nominally the EU's strategic AI plan, but operational priorities have shifted to AI Act implementation, GPAI compliance, AI Factories, and the Apply AI Strategy (2025). A comprehensive revision of the Coordinated Plan has not been published.

For UK firms, the practical implications today are:

1. **Treat the Plan as historical context, not current operational guidance.** AI Act implementation, GPAI compliance, and the Apply AI Strategy are more immediately relevant.

2. **Reference the Plan's sectoral priorities for funding alignment.** Horizon Europe, Digital Europe, EHDS-related calls, and AI Factories funding tend to align with the Plan's 17 action areas.

3. **Understand the Plan's political frame.** "Trustworthy, human-centric, sustainable, inclusive" AI remains the EU's positional framing in international dialogue. Firms engaging EU policymakers or partners benefit from this vocabulary.

4. **Note what the Plan did not anticipate**: GPAI, foundation models, agentic AI, systemic risks. These have moved to the centre of EU AI policy since 2022-2023, with the Coordinated Plan's framework adapted piecemeal rather than comprehensively revised.

The Plan is useful as a historical reference and as a source of investment and infrastructure context. For regulatory compliance work, it is background. For investment planning, it is still directionally relevant — particularly its sectoral priorities. For UK-based consultants advising EU clients, the Plan is necessary reading to understand the EU's AI policy architecture and the distinction between the binding AI Act and the broader strategic framework.
