# EU AI Pact — Review

**Document:** EU AI Pact — Press Release (IP/24/4864) and associated voluntary pledges
**Published:** 25 September 2024 (first signatory announcement)
**Issuing body:** European Commission / EU AI Office
**Legal status:** Voluntary; not binding; signatories opt in to commitments
**Purpose:** Bridge mechanism between AI Act entry into force (1 August 2024) and full application (2 August 2026), encouraging early voluntary compliance

**Initial announcement:** Over 100 first signatories, including multinationals and SMEs from IT, telecoms, healthcare, banking, automotive, aeronautics

---

## Summary

The EU AI Pact is a **voluntary compliance-anticipation initiative** launched by the European Commission through the AI Office. It invites companies to sign up to structured pledges to begin applying AI Act principles before the Act becomes fully applicable on 2 August 2026. The first signatory cohort (25 September 2024) comprised over 100 companies across sectors. The Pact establishes three **core commitments**: (1) develop an AI governance strategy, (2) map AI systems likely to qualify as high-risk, (3) promote AI literacy. More than half of signatories also opted into **additional pledges**, including human oversight, risk mitigation, and transparent labelling of AI-generated content such as deepfakes. The Pact is part of a broader Commission AI innovation package alongside the AI Factories initiative (10 September 2024), GenAI4EU, the Large AI Grand Challenge, and the forthcoming European AI Research Council.

---

## Key Points

### The Three Core Pledges

Companies signing the Pact commit to at least:

1. **AI governance strategy** — foster the uptake of AI within the organisation and work toward future AI Act compliance
2. **High-risk AI systems mapping** — identify AI systems likely to be categorised as high-risk under the AI Act (i.e., Annex III systems or safety-component systems under Annex I)
3. **AI literacy and awareness** — promote staff training and responsible AI development

These align with AI Act provisions:
- Article 4 (AI literacy — applicable from 2 February 2025)
- Chapter III, Section 2 (high-risk obligations — applicable from 2 August 2026)
- Article 17 (quality management system — high-risk providers)

### Additional Voluntary Pledges

More than half of first-wave signatories added further commitments:

- **Human oversight** (Article 14 — high-risk AI systems)
- **Risk mitigation** (Article 9 — risk management system for high-risk AI)
- **Transparent labelling of AI-generated content** (Article 50 — transparency obligations, including deepfakes)

### Relationship to the AI Act Timeline

The AI Act entered into force on 1 August 2024. Application dates:

- **2 February 2025**: Prohibitions (Article 5) and AI literacy (Article 4) apply
- **2 August 2025**: Governance rules, GPAI obligations (Chapter V), penalties, and notification requirements apply
- **2 August 2026**: Full applicability (high-risk obligations, transparency obligations — Article 50, etc.)
- **2 August 2027**: Rules for AI systems embedded in regulated products (Annex I safety components)

The Pact serves as a **voluntary early-adoption bridge** for the 2024-2026 transition period, enabling organisations to start building compliance infrastructure before the binding obligations take effect.

### Broader Commission AI Innovation Package

Announced alongside the Pact:

- **AI Factories initiative** (10 September 2024) — one-stop shops for startups and industry combining data, talent, and computing power from EuroHPC-JU supercomputers; applications span healthcare, energy, automotive, transport, defence, aerospace, robotics, manufacturing, clean tech, agritech
- **GenAI4EU initiative** — deploying generative AI in key European sectors
- **Large AI Grand Challenge** — financial support for startups plus access to EU supercomputers
- **European AI Research Council** — planned to exploit potential of data
- **Apply AI Strategy** — boost new industrial uses of AI (later published in 2025)
- **Common European Data Spaces** — cross-sector data sharing infrastructure
- **Venture capital and equity support measures** — startup financing

### Stakeholder Engagement Architecture

The Pact is not merely a compliance instrument but also an **engagement mechanism** between the AI Office and:

- Industry (multinationals, SMEs)
- Civil society
- Academia
- Member State authorities

Open for continued enrolment until the AI Act is fully applicable. Companies can join at any time and commit to core + additional pledges.

### Signatory Sector Distribution (first wave)

- IT
- Telecoms
- Healthcare
- Banking
- Automotive
- Aeronautics
- Others (specific list at the EU AI Pact signatories page)

The first wave covered 100+ companies. The geographic and sectoral diversity was explicitly highlighted — both multinationals and SMEs were represented.

## Observations

### Clarity

The Pact is simply structured — three core commitments plus optional add-ons. The short press release format conveys the main architecture. Clarity strengths:

- Three-pledge structure is easy to communicate and internally implement
- Alignment with specific AI Act articles is implicit but traceable
- Voluntary nature avoids legal complexity

Clarity weaknesses:

- The press release does not specify **audit or verification** mechanisms. How does the Commission verify signatory claims?
- The boundary between the three core pledges and the additional pledges is unclear — are signatories listed publicly with their specific commitments?
- The Pact does not define **failure to comply** consequences (other than reputational)

### Measurability

Minimal. The Pact is a commitment framework, not a measurement framework. No thresholds, deadlines (beyond AI Act milestones), or KPIs:

- "Governance strategy" — not defined
- "Mapping of high-risk AI systems" — not defined
- "AI literacy" — not quantified (no training hours, coverage percentages)
- "Human oversight" — not specified (in contrast to Article 14 in the AI Act)
- "Transparent labelling" — not specified (though Article 50 and the Transparency Code later provide specification)

This is intentional: the Pact is a voluntary declaration of intent; the measurable obligations come via the AI Act itself and its secondary instruments (GPAI Code of Practice, Transparency Code, Serious Incident Reporting Guidance).

### Gaps

**No enforcement mechanism.** Voluntary nature means no sanctions for non-compliance. This is consistent with the Pact's purpose but means the Pact alone cannot drive change without the AI Act backstop.

**No public performance reporting.** Signatories commit but the Commission does not publish ongoing performance assessments of the pledge-fulfilment.

**Unclear SME support.** The Pact emphasises SME inclusion but does not specify how SMEs can access the core commitments proportionate to their capacity (unlike the GPAI Code of Practice, which has explicit SMC/SME provisions).

**No GPAI-specific track.** GPAI provider obligations (Article 55, 56) are not mentioned. The Pact focuses on AI system deployment rather than GPAI model provision. Signatories who are GPAI providers should separately consider the GPAI Code of Practice.

**No specific deepfake content guidance.** The Pact mentions "transparently labelling certain types of AI-generated content, such as deepfakes" as an additional pledge, but the operational specification was left to the subsequent Transparency Code (first draft December 2025).

### Feasibility

Highly feasible for large companies with existing compliance and governance functions. The three core pledges require:

1. Internal governance policy document (low cost)
2. Inventory of AI systems (medium effort; SaaS and third-party AI complicates this)
3. AI literacy training programme (medium cost)

For SMEs, feasibility depends on the Pact's interpretation — a small firm with 10 AI-using processes may find mapping easier than a large firm with 1,000 processes. The Pact's flexible structure (no deadlines beyond AI Act dates) makes initial signatory commitment low-risk.

### Internal Consistency

The Pact is consistent internally. The three pledges map to AI Act articles:

- Pledge 1 (governance) → Article 17 (QMS for high-risk) + general AI Act architecture
- Pledge 2 (mapping) → Annex III + Annex I definition of high-risk
- Pledge 3 (literacy) → Article 4

The additional pledges map to Articles 14, 9, and 50.

### Coverage

The Pact covers the transition period (August 2024 - August 2026) and provides a **soft-launch mechanism** for AI Act implementation. It does not cover:

- GPAI provider obligations (separate: GPAI Code of Practice)
- Transparency specifics (separate: Transparency Code)
- Serious incident reporting (separate: Article 73 guidance)
- Prohibited practices (separate: Commission Guidelines)

The Pact is therefore the **"umbrella commitment" layer** above specialised voluntary codes.

### International Alignment

The Pact is EU-specific. However, the principles align with:

- OECD AI Principles (2019)
- G7 Hiroshima AI Process Code of Conduct
- NIST AI Risk Management Framework (US)
- UK AI Safety Institute work on frontier model safety

For multinational firms with EU operations, the Pact is typically part of a broader global AI governance framework. Signing the Pact is often a low-cost signal of engagement rather than a stand-alone compliance commitment.

### Strategic Signalling

The Pact has significant **symbolic weight**:

1. **Industry buy-in demonstration** — 100+ companies signing in the first tranche signals broad industry acceptance of the AI Act approach
2. **Commission-industry dialogue** — ongoing engagement channel with the AI Office, useful for influencing secondary instruments (guidelines, codes)
3. **Early-mover positioning** — signatories demonstrate commitment before the compliance deadline
4. **Reputation and ESG alignment** — AI Pact signatory status can support ESG reporting and stakeholder communication

---

## Overall Assessment

The EU AI Pact is a **lightweight, voluntary bridge instrument** linking the AI Act's entry into force (August 2024) to its full application (August 2026). Its three core pledges mirror AI Act obligations without imposing binding commitments. It is designed primarily as a **signalling and engagement mechanism** — demonstrating industry cooperation, facilitating Commission-industry dialogue, and building implementation momentum.

For UK-based firms with EU operations, the Pact is relevant for three reasons:

1. **Symbolic value**: Signing the Pact demonstrates commitment to responsible AI and alignment with EU values. Useful for EU stakeholder relations, procurement positioning, and ESG reporting.

2. **Early compliance infrastructure**: The three pledges are compliance steps firms will need regardless. Treating them as a pre-binding commitment structure creates momentum before the August 2026 deadline.

3. **Access to the AI Office dialogue**: Signatories participate in the Commission-industry dialogue structure, which shapes secondary instruments (GPAI Code of Practice, Transparency Code, etc.).

The Pact's greatest strength is **low barrier to entry with meaningful symbolic weight**. Its greatest weakness is **lack of verification and enforcement** — signing is easy but performance is not independently audited, creating potential for signatory claims to outpace implementation reality.

Practical recommendations for UK firms:

1. **If you provide or deploy AI systems for EU users, consider signing.** The three core pledges are low-cost and align with AI Act compliance you will eventually need to implement.

2. **If you provide GPAI models for EU users, the Pact is secondary.** The primary voluntary route is the GPAI Code of Practice (signed by 24 firms as of July-October 2025, including Anthropic, Google, OpenAI, Microsoft, and Mistral).

3. **If you operate AI systems in high-risk Annex III categories, use Pledge 2 (mapping) as a compliance kick-off.** The mapping exercise is the first step toward Article 6 classification and Article 17 quality management system setup.

4. **If you deploy deepfakes or AI-generated content on matters of public interest, the Pact's additional pledge on labelling is a relevant early commitment.** Full specification comes via the Transparency Code (first draft December 2025, final expected June 2026).

5. **Track the signatory list.** The Commission maintains a public list of signatories with their specific commitments. Peer and competitor positioning may inform corporate decisions.

The Pact is **not** a substitute for AI Act compliance. It is a pre-compliance framework. Its main value lies in the transition period; after August 2026, the binding obligations apply regardless of Pact participation. Firms should view the Pact as a scaffolding for compliance rather than an alternative to it.
