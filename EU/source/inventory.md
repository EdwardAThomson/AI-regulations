## AI Act Implementation Timeline

- **2 Feb 2025** — Prohibitions (Article 5), AI literacy (Article 4), definitions — **in force**
- **2 Aug 2025** — General-purpose AI (GPAI) model rules — **in force**
- **2 Aug 2026** — Enforcement of GPAI rules; high-risk AI system rules; transparency obligations (Article 50); governance and penalties
- **2 Aug 2027** — High-risk AI systems falling under Annex I product safety legislation

## Notes

- **The EU AI Act is the centrepiece.** Phased application runs through August 2027.
  - Prohibited practices + AI literacy: in force Feb 2025
  - GPAI rules: in force Aug 2025 (enforcement from Aug 2026)
  - High-risk system provisions: Aug 2026
- **The AI Liability Directive has been formally withdrawn** (Feb 2025). The Commission cited lack of agreement among stakeholders. A replacement under a different approach is possible but not proposed.
- **The GPAI Code of Practice final version** (July 2025) is a major compliance document for providers of general-purpose AI models — structured around three chapters (transparency, copyright, safety/security). It is voluntary but serves as the primary route for demonstrating compliance with the Act's GPAI obligations.
- **Serious Incident Reporting guidance** (draft Sep 2025) introduces tiered timelines: 2 days for widespread infringement or critical infrastructure disruption; 10 days for death; 15 days for other serious incidents. Applies from Aug 2026.
- **Transparency Code of Practice for AI-Generated Content** (Article 50) is being developed through 2025-2026. First draft Dec 2025; final Jun 2026.
- Adjacent legislation (GDPR, DSA, Data Act) is relevant but secondary — prioritise AI-specific instruments first.
- The Council of Europe Framework Convention on AI (Vilnius, 2024) is international, not EU-specific, but the EU and several Member States are signatories.
- **UK relevance:** The Act applies extraterritorially. UK-established providers whose AI systems are placed on the EU market, or whose outputs are used in the EU, fall within scope regardless of UK establishment. This is the primary basis on which UK-based firms serving EU clients need to understand the EU framework.
