# National AI Strategy 2.0 — Review

**Document:** National AI Strategy 2.0: AI for the Public Good, for Singapore and the World
**Published:** December 2023
**Issuing body:** Smart Nation and Digital Government Group (SNDGG), Singapore
**Legal status:** Non-binding national strategy
**Length:** ~65 pages
**Predecessor:** National AI Strategy 1.0 (November 2019)
**Foreword:** Lawrence Wong, Deputy Prime Minister & Minister for Finance

---

## Summary

Singapore's second National AI Strategy sets the country's AI ambitions for the next 3-5 years (2024-2028), framed around the vision of "AI for the Public Good, for Singapore and the World." The strategy is organised into **three systems** with **10 enablers** and **15 actions**: (1) Activity Drivers (industry, government, research), (2) People & Communities (talent, capabilities, placemaking), and (3) Infrastructure & Environment (compute, data, trusted environment, international leadership). The strategy makes three explicit shifts from NAIS 1.0: from opportunity to necessity, from local to global, and from projects to systems. It identifies peaks of excellence across leading economic sectors (manufacturing, financial services, transport/logistics, biomedical), Smart Nation priorities (healthcare, education, trust & safety, public service), and cross-cutting capabilities (AI for business operations, AI for science, foundational AI — specifically reasoning AI, resource-efficient AI, and responsible AI).

---

## Key Points

### Vision and Twin Goals

- **Vision**: AI for the Public Good, for Singapore and the World
- **Goal 1 — Excellence**: selectively develop peaks of excellence in AI to advance the field and maximise value creation
- **Goal 2 — Empowerment**: raise up individuals, businesses, and communities to use AI with confidence, discernment, and trust

### Three Strategic Shifts from NAIS 1.0

1. **Opportunity → Necessity**: AI skills are a "must know," not "good to have"
2. **Local → Global**: Singapore-linked talent and businesses should aspire to be world-leading
3. **Projects → Systems**: move beyond flagship National AI Projects to a systems approach coordinating stakeholders at scale

### Three Systems, 10 Enablers, 15 Actions

#### System 1 — Activity Drivers

**Enabler 1: Industry**
- Action 1: Anchor new AI Centres of Excellence (CoEs) and explore Sectoral AI CoEs in key sectors
- Action 2: Strengthen AI start-up ecosystem; attract AI-focused accelerator programmes

**Enabler 2: Government**
- Action 3: Improve public service productivity via AI; Smart Nation priority sectors (healthcare, education) develop sector-specific AI strategies; whole-of-government functional domains (finance, HR, service delivery) optimise with AI

**Enabler 3: Research**
- Action 4: Update national AI R&D plans — five dimensions: research priorities, industry-academia nexus, talent, compute, international collaboration. Three research themes: Reasoning AI, Resource-efficient AI, Responsible AI

#### System 2 — People & Communities

Three tiers of AI talent: Creators (top-tier researchers/engineers), Practitioners (tech workers scaling solutions), Users (enterprises and general workforce)

**Enabler 4: Talent**
- Action 5: Attract world's top AI Creators with dedicated team, bespoke support, hybrid working arrangements
- Action 6: Deepen AI Practitioner pipeline — expand university places, conversion programmes (TeSA, AIAP), school programmes

**Enabler 5: Capabilities**
- Action 7: Equip enterprises with AI capabilities — AI readiness framework, Enterprise AI support, acceleration programmes for different maturity levels
- Action 8: Equip workers with AI competencies — AI literacy for general workforce; "AI for Everyone" curriculum; sector-specific upskilling

**Enabler 6: Placemaking**
- Action 9: Create a focal site for AI community — physical hub for researchers, entrepreneurs, students; digital community layer for remote participation; regular AI events (RAISE.SG, Neural Networking series, Singapore Conference on AI)

#### System 3 — Infrastructure & Environment

**Enabler 7: Compute**
- Action 10: Significantly increase high-performance compute — deepen partnerships with chipmakers and CSPs; allocate carbon budget and power for data centres; manage subset of GPUs for meritorious use cases and public good

**Enabler 8: Data**
- Action 11: Build capabilities in data services and PETs — synthetic data generation, data annotation, federated learning, homomorphic encryption; PET Sandbox for industry experimentation
- Action 12: Unlock government data for public good — "data concierge" within government to broker access for AI developers

**Enabler 9: Trusted Environment**
- Action 13: Ensure fit-for-purpose regulatory environment — update Model AI Governance Framework and AI Verify for GenAI; risk-based, tiered, sector-adapted interventions; establish common platform for regulatory coordination; safety R&D including alignment and evaluations; translating guidelines into technical standards

**Enabler 10: Leader in Thought and Action**
- Action 14: Champion international AI governance norms — contribute to GPAI, OECD, UN, ASEAN, and bilateral partnerships; promote Singapore's governance frameworks internationally
- Action 15: Step up Singapore's contributions to international conversations on safety and security

### Pre-existing Achievements (cited)

- S$500M+ invested through AI Singapore (AISG) under RIE 2020 and 2025
- 80+ active AI research faculty
- 150 AI R&D and product teams
- 1,100 AI start-ups in Singapore
- Ranked 1st per capita in Stanford Global AI Vibrancy Index (2021); 2nd in Oxford Insights Government AI Index (2021); 3rd in Tortoise Media Global AI Index (2022)
- Model AI Governance Framework (2019, world's first)
- AI Verify (2022, open-sourced June 2023; 90+ corporate members by December 2023)
- Active in GPAI, WEF AI Governance Alliance, UN High-Level Advisory Body on AI

### Compute and Sustainability

Singapore recognises the tension between AI compute needs and its sustainability goals. The strategy commits to:
- Allocating sufficient carbon budget and power for data centres
- Charting a roadmap toward net-zero, green data centres powered by renewable energy
- Collaborating with industry on innovative sustainability proposals
- Careful resource management given Singapore's land, power, water, and carbon constraints

### Governance Positioning

The strategy explicitly endorses:
- **Pro-innovation regulatory approach** with appropriate guardrails
- **Risk-based, tiered, sector-adapted** governance interventions
- **Voluntary frameworks** (Model AI Governance Framework, AI Verify) as primary governance tools
- **No standalone AI legislation** — existing laws (PDPA, sector regulators) remain the binding framework
- **AI Verify Foundation** as the international testing community anchor (11 principles, standardised tests, open-source toolkit)

## Observations

### Clarity

Well-structured: three systems → 10 enablers → 15 actions. Illustrated with feature stories (American Express, Grab, OneService Chatbot, Pair, Q&M Dental, Singapore Conference on AI, PET Sandbox). The three strategic shifts provide a clear narrative of evolution from NAIS 1.0.

Clarity strengths:
- Three-tier talent framework (Creators / Practitioners / Users) is memorable and actionable
- Peaks of excellence concept focuses resource allocation
- Specific investment figures and international rankings ground the narrative in evidence
- Feature stories demonstrate real-world implementation

Clarity weaknesses:
- 15 actions are described at high level; implementation detail is deferred
- No budgets attached to individual actions (contrast with UK AI Opportunities Action Plan which attached specific sums)
- Timelines are "next 3-5 years" rather than specific milestones

### Measurability

Limited. The strategy cites baseline metrics (80+ AI faculty, 1,100 startups, S$500M+ invested) but does not set quantitative targets. No KPIs for any of the 15 actions. The three strategic shifts are qualitative.

### Gaps

**No budgets for individual actions.** Aggregate figures (S$500M+ through AISG) are cited but not allocated to specific strategy actions.

**No binding regulatory commitments.** Governance remains voluntary. Action 13 ("ensure fit-for-purpose regulatory environment") commits only to updating voluntary frameworks.

**GPAI / foundation model governance light.** The strategy mentions AI models becoming "more powerful and accessible" but does not address the specific governance challenges of foundation models (compute thresholds, downstream deployment chains, systemic risk). The subsequent GenAI Framework (June 2024) and Agentic AI Framework (January 2026) fill this gap.

**Labour market disruption acknowledged but lightly treated.** The strategy focuses on upskilling and new opportunities but does not address displacement scenarios, transition support, or worker protections.

**Compute sustainability tension acknowledged but unresolved.** The strategy commits to allocating carbon budget for data centres and charting a net-zero roadmap, but does not specify how the competing demands of AI compute growth and sustainability targets will be balanced.

### Feasibility

Feasible for a well-resourced city-state with strong institutional capacity. Singapore's track record on NAIS 1.0 delivery (AISG, AI Verify, Governance Framework, international positioning) supports confidence in execution. The 15 actions are appropriately scoped for government coordination — they are enabling actions (attract, coordinate, fund, regulate) rather than direct delivery.

### International Alignment

Explicitly positioned in the international landscape:
- **GPAI** — founding member; active in working groups
- **OECD** — active contributor to AI principles and governance work
- **UN** — High-Level Advisory Body on AI participation
- **ASEAN** — guide development (ASEAN Guide on AI Governance, 2024)
- **Bilateral** — Singapore-Republic of Korea joint AI R&D; partnerships with UK, US, Japan on safety institutes
- **AI Verify Foundation** — international open-source community for AI governance testing

---

## Overall Assessment

NAIS 2.0 is a **coherent, well-structured national strategy** that positions Singapore as an AI leader relative to its size. The three-systems architecture, three strategic shifts, and 15 actions provide a clear organisational framework. The strategy's distinctive strength is its combination of **ambition** (peaks of excellence, global leadership) with **pragmatism** (voluntary governance, sector-specific interventions, resource constraints acknowledged).

For UK-based firms:

1. **Singapore is a key Asia-Pacific AI governance reference point.** Its voluntary frameworks (Model AI Governance, GenAI Framework, Agentic AI Framework, AI Verify) are the most developed voluntary AI governance stack globally.

2. **Peaks of excellence in financial services, healthcare, and biomedical are relevant to UK firms operating in APAC.** Regulatory alignment (MAS FEAT Principles, PDPC guidance) and governance alignment (Model Framework) are expected.

3. **The AI Verify Foundation provides practical testing infrastructure** — open-source governance testing toolkit. UK firms can use AI Verify to validate AI system performance against internationally recognised principles.

4. **Singapore's approach contrasts with both EU and UK**: more operationally specific than the UK's principles-only framework; less binding than the EU's statutory approach; uniquely complemented by practical testing infrastructure.
