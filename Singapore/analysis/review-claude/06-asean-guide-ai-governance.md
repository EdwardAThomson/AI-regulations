# ASEAN Guide on AI Governance and Ethics (2024) — Review

**Document:** ASEAN Guide on AI Governance and Ethics
**Published:** 2024 (endorsed at the 4th ASEAN Digital Ministers Meeting)
**Issuing body:** ASEAN, facilitated by Singapore (IMDA/PDPC) as lead shepherd
**Legal status:** Non-binding regional guide
**Length:** ~85 pages (including annexes and use cases)
**Audience:** Organisations in ASEAN designing, developing, or deploying traditional AI in commercial and non-military applications

---

## Summary

The ASEAN Guide extends Singapore's Model AI Governance Framework to the 10-member ASEAN region. It adopts the same **four-component structure** (internal governance, human involvement in decision-making, operations management, stakeholder interaction) and adds **seven guiding principles** (transparency/explainability, fairness/equity, security/safety, human-centricity, privacy/data governance, accountability/integrity, robustness/reliability). National-level and regional-level recommendations are included. An AI Risk Impact Assessment Template (Annex A) provides a practical tool for organisations to evaluate AI system risks. Six use cases from ASEAN organisations (Aboitiz Group, EY, Gojek, UCARE.AI, Singapore Smart Nation Group, Singapore Ministry of Education) demonstrate implementation.

---

## Key Points

### Seven Guiding Principles

1. **Transparency and Explainability** — AI decisions should be explainable to affected stakeholders
2. **Fairness and Equity** — AI should not create or reinforce unfair bias
3. **Security and Safety** — AI systems should be resilient and secure
4. **Human-centricity** — human wellbeing and interests are paramount
5. **Privacy and Data Governance** — personal data protected; data governance maintained
6. **Accountability and Integrity** — clear roles and responsibilities; organisational accountability
7. **Robustness and Reliability** — AI systems should perform reliably under expected conditions

### Four-Component Governance Framework

Mirrors Singapore's 2020 Model Framework:
1. Internal governance structures and measures (ethics board, roles/responsibilities, risk management)
2. Determining the level of human involvement (HITL / HOTL / HOOTL based on risk)
3. Operations management (data lifecycle, model development, monitoring, bias mitigation)
4. Stakeholder interaction and communication (disclosure, feedback, employee adaptation)

### National-Level Recommendations

- Nurturing AI talent and upskilling workforce
- Supporting AI innovation ecosystem and start-up investment
- Investing in AI R&D (including cybersecurity of AI, governance, ethics)
- Developing AI governance frameworks with multi-stakeholder engagement
- Enabling trusted cross-border data flows

### Regional-Level Recommendations

- Promoting interoperability of AI governance frameworks across ASEAN
- Facilitating cross-border data flows within ASEAN
- Collaborative AI R&D and talent development
- Shared approaches to AI standards and testing

### AI Risk Impact Assessment Template (Annex A)

A practical 13-page template covering:
- AI system description and intended use
- Risk assessment across the seven principles
- Mitigation measures for identified risks
- Human oversight arrangements
- Monitoring and review plans

This is one of the few publicly available, structured AI risk assessment templates from any jurisdiction — genuinely useful for organisations beginning AI governance.

## Observations

### Significance

The ASEAN Guide is the primary regional AI governance reference for Southeast Asia (~680 million people across 10 countries). It provides a common vocabulary and framework for jurisdictions at very different levels of AI maturity (Singapore at one end; Myanmar and Laos at the other). Singapore's shepherding role gives the guide credibility and coherence.

### Relationship to Singapore's Framework

The Guide is essentially Singapore's 2020 Model Framework regionalised — same four components, similar principles, compatible risk-assessment approach. This is deliberate: Singapore designed its domestic framework with ASEAN adoption in mind. The Guide adds regional and national recommendations that Singapore's domestic framework does not address.

### Gaps

- Covers traditional AI only (2024 publication); the Expanded Guide (January 2025) addresses GenAI
- Non-binding across all ASEAN members — no enforcement mechanism
- Risk assessment template is useful but application varies enormously by member-state capacity
- No prohibited practices, no risk tiers, no incident reporting
