# Expanded ASEAN Guide on AI Governance and Ethics — Generative AI (2025) — Review

**Document:** Expanded ASEAN Guide on AI Governance and Ethics — Generative AI
**Published:** January 2025
**Issuing body:** ASEAN, facilitated by Singapore (IMDA/AI Verify Foundation)
**Legal status:** Non-binding regional guide
**Length:** ~50 pages (including use cases and methodology appendix)
**Supplements:** ASEAN Guide on AI Governance and Ethics (2024)

---

## Summary

This Expanded Guide supplements the 2024 ASEAN Guide with GenAI-specific policy considerations. It adopts the **same nine-dimension structure** as Singapore's Model AI Governance Framework for Generative AI (June 2024) — Accountability, Data, Trusted Development and Deployment, Incident Reporting, Testing and Assurance, Security, Content Provenance, Safety and Alignment R&D, AI for Public Good — and contextualises each for the ASEAN region. The Guide identifies six GenAI-specific risks (mistakes/anthropomorphism, factual inaccuracy/disinformation, deepfakes/impersonation/fraud, IP infringement, privacy/confidentiality, embedded biases) and notes frontier/systemic risks for future consideration. Four use cases illustrate implementation: PhoGPT (VinAI, Vietnam), Project Moonshot (AI Verify Foundation, Singapore), Accenture's Responsible AI programme (ASEAN-wide), and ThaiLLM (Thailand).

---

## Key Points

### Six GenAI-Specific Risks

1. **Mistakes and anthropomorphism** — users attributing human-like understanding to AI
2. **Factually inaccurate responses and disinformation** — hallucination and misinformation at scale
3. **Deepfakes, impersonation, fraudulent and malicious activities** — synthetic media misuse
4. **Infringement of intellectual property rights** — training data copyright; output attribution
5. **Privacy and confidentiality** — personal data in training; user data in prompts
6. **Propagation of embedded biases** — amplification of training data biases

Plus acknowledgement of **frontier and systemic risks** (not widespread in ASEAN currently but requiring future consideration) — paralleling the EU's four specified systemic risks but without specifying categories.

### Nine Policy Dimensions

Identical structure to Singapore's GenAI Framework:

1. **Accountability** — shared responsibility across the AI development chain
2. **Data** — trusted use of personal data; copyright balance; quality data access
3. **Trusted Development and Deployment** — baseline safety practices; "food labels" disclosure; evaluation
4. **Incident Reporting** — vulnerability reporting; proportionate incident reporting; reference to EU AI Act approach
5. **Testing and Assurance** — standardised testing; third-party accreditation
6. **Security** — adapted security-by-design; new safeguards for GenAI threat vectors
7. **Content Provenance** — digital watermarking; cryptographic provenance (C2PA); publisher cooperation
8. **Safety and Alignment R&D** — forward/backward alignment; AI Safety Institute cooperation
9. **AI for Public Good** — democratising access; public service; workforce; sustainability

### Use Cases

- **PhoGPT** (VinAI, Vietnam) — Vietnamese-language large language model; illustrates local-language AI development
- **Project Moonshot** (AI Verify Foundation, Singapore) — LLM red-teaming toolkit; practical testing infrastructure
- **Accenture Responsible AI** (ASEAN-wide) — enterprise responsible AI programme across the region
- **ThaiLLM** (BDI/NSTDA/VISTEC, Thailand) — Thai-language LLM consortium; parallels Iceland's language preservation concern

### Regional Emphasis

The Guide emphasises:
- **Interoperability** of governance frameworks across ASEAN's diverse regulatory landscapes
- **Pro-innovation** approach with proportional safety measures
- **Local-language AI development** as a regional priority (Vietnamese, Thai, Bahasa, etc.)
- **Coordinated regional response** rather than fragmented national approaches

## Observations

### Singapore's Regional Influence

The Guide confirms Singapore's role as ASEAN's AI governance architect. The nine-dimension structure is lifted directly from Singapore's domestic GenAI Framework. Use cases feature Singaporean organisations and infrastructure (AI Verify Foundation, Project Moonshot). The intellectual DNA is Singaporean; the application is regional.

### Comparison with EU

The ASEAN Expanded Guide references the EU AI Act (specifically Article 73 on incident reporting) as one reference point among several. It does not adopt the EU's risk-tier approach, prohibited practices, or binding enforcement. The relationship is "learn from" rather than "align with" — ASEAN maintains its voluntary, pro-innovation posture.

### Low-Resource Language AI

The use cases (PhoGPT for Vietnamese, ThaiLLM for Thai) highlight a concern shared with Iceland and other small-language-community jurisdictions: if governance frameworks don't account for local-language AI development, the technology may not serve these populations. The Expanded Guide positions local-language AI as both an innovation opportunity and a governance priority — practical and culturally significant.

### Gaps

- Non-binding across all 10 ASEAN members — no enforcement mechanism
- Frontier/systemic risks acknowledged but not categorised or operationalised
- No compute thresholds, no risk tiers, no prohibited practices
- Implementation capacity varies enormously across ASEAN (Singapore vs Cambodia, for example)
