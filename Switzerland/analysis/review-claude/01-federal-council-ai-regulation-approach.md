# Federal Council AI Regulation Approach — Review

**Document:** Federal Council Decision on AI Regulation Approach (media release)
**Published:** 12 February 2025
**Issuing body:** Swiss Federal Council
**Legal status:** Policy announcement; non-binding; mandates FDJP to draft legislation by end 2026
**Length:** ~1 page (media release; supplemented by background reporting)
**Background:** Follows the November 2023 Federal Council mandate to DETEC and FDFA to prepare an overview of possible regulatory approaches to AI. That earlier mandate produced a baseline legal analysis, sectoral analysis, and comparative international analysis, which informed this February 2025 decision.

---

## Summary

The Swiss Federal Council decided on 12 February 2025 to reject a comprehensive, cross-sector AI Act and instead pursue a **sector-specific regulatory model** anchored by ratification of the **Council of Europe Framework Convention on Artificial Intelligence** (the Vilnius Convention, September 2024). The Convention will be incorporated into Swiss law primarily affecting state actors, while sector-specific adjustments to existing laws will address areas affecting fundamental rights (transparency, data protection, non-discrimination, supervision). The private sector will face limited additional obligations. The Federal Department of Justice and Police (FDJP) will draft a bill for public consultation by **end of 2026**, and DETEC (together with FDJP, FDFA, and EAER) will develop an implementation plan by the same date for measures not requiring legislative changes. The approach explicitly avoids what officials term a "Swiss finish" — excessive regulatory burden relative to trading partners.

---

## Key Points

### The Decision: No Swiss AI Act

Switzerland will **not** adopt a comprehensive horizontal AI Act equivalent to the EU AI Act. The Federal Council considered and rejected this option following the November 2023 mandate, which had tasked the Federal Administration with assessing regulatory approaches including:

- Continuing current sector-specific regulation only
- No overarching federal AI regulation beyond existing frameworks
- A horizontal AI Act (EU-style)

The Council chose a hybrid: sector-specific adjustments plus limited cross-sectoral provisions anchored in the Council of Europe Convention.

### Three Pillars of the Swiss Approach

1. **Council of Europe AI Convention implementation** — Switzerland will ratify and incorporate the Convention into Swiss law. The Convention applies primarily to state actors and requires signatories to ensure AI systems respect human rights, democracy, and the rule of law throughout their lifecycle. Unlike the EU AI Act, the Convention does not prescribe specific prohibitions or risk tiers; it obligates states to assess whether regulation is necessary for their context.

2. **Sector-specific legislative adjustments** — Changes will be made within existing legal frameworks where needed, concentrated on areas affecting fundamental rights:
   - Stricter transparency requirements
   - Mandatory labelling of AI-generated content
   - Political advertising regulations on social media
   - Enhanced data protection (facial recognition restrictions, information obligations)
   - Extended privacy impact assessment obligations
   - Targeted rules for finance, healthcare, and digital platforms
   - Non-discrimination and equality protections

3. **Non-binding complementary measures** — Self-disclosure agreements, industry-developed solutions, and voluntary frameworks alongside legislation.

### Timeline

- **FDJP** will draft a bill for public consultation by **end of 2026** implementing the Council of Europe AI Convention
- **DETEC** (with FDJP, FDFA, EAER) will develop an **implementation plan by end of 2026** for measures not requiring legislative changes
- The implementation plan will specifically consider alignment with key trading partners (i.e., the EU)

### Strategic Objectives

The Federal Council's approach aims to:

1. **Reinforce Switzerland as a centre of innovation** — regulatory environment should attract rather than deter AI development
2. **Safeguard fundamental rights, including economic freedom** — "economic freedom" is explicitly listed alongside traditional fundamental rights, signalling the Federal Council's innovation-permissive posture
3. **Increase public trust in AI** — through proportionate, transparent regulation
4. **Maintain competitiveness relative to key trading partners** — the EU equivalence question is implicit but central

### Context: The November 2023 Mandate

The February 2025 decision rests on analysis commissioned in November 2023, when the Federal Council mandated DETEC and FDFA to prepare:

- A **baseline legal analysis** of Swiss law's existing coverage of AI risks
- A **sectoral analysis** identifying gaps in current regulatory frameworks
- A **comparative international analysis** of AI regulation in other countries

This analysis reportedly found that Swiss law already covers many AI-related risks through existing frameworks (data protection, consumer protection, product liability, competition, financial market regulation, medical device regulation), though gaps exist in transparency, non-discrimination, and oversight of AI in public administration.

## Observations

### Clarity

The decision is clearly stated: no Swiss AI Act; sector-specific plus Convention implementation. The three-pillar structure is legible.

Clarity weaknesses:
- The media release is brief. The underlying analysis (November 2023 mandate output) has not been published in full English translation, making it difficult to assess the depth of the sectoral and legal analysis.
- "Sector-specific legislative adjustments" are listed at headline level but without specification of which existing laws will be amended, in what way, or on what timeline beyond "end of 2026."
- The boundary between binding legislative changes and non-binding complementary measures is not precisely drawn.

### Measurability

Minimal. The only hard deadline is "end of 2026" for the FDJP bill and the DETEC implementation plan. No interim milestones, no measurable targets for regulatory coverage, no compliance benchmarks.

### Gaps

**EU equivalence is acknowledged but not addressed.** The implementation plan will "specifically consider Switzerland's alignment with key trading partners" but the decision does not state whether Swiss law will aim for EU AI Act equivalence, adequacy, or simply diplomatic compatibility. For Swiss companies exporting to the EU, the AI Act applies directly to their EU-market-facing activities regardless of domestic Swiss regulation. The domestic Swiss framework's real function is governance of domestic AI use and Switzerland's international reputation.

**No risk classification.** Unlike the EU (prohibited / high-risk / limited / minimal) or even the UK (sector-regulator context-specific), Switzerland does not establish any AI risk taxonomy. Individual sectors will make their own assessments.

**No GPAI / foundation model provisions.** The decision does not address general-purpose AI models, compute thresholds, systemic risk, or the distinctive regulatory challenges of foundation models. This is a significant gap given that Swiss firms (notably Google DeepMind Zurich, ETH spin-offs, and others) participate in frontier AI development.

**No prohibited practices.** Switzerland does not identify any AI use that should be prohibited outright. The EU's Article 5 prohibitions (social scoring, untargeted biometric scraping, emotion recognition in workplace/education) have no Swiss equivalent. Whether the Council of Europe Convention's human-rights obligations would require Switzerland to prohibit some practices remains to be determined during the FDJP drafting process.

**No enforcement architecture.** The media release does not specify which authority will enforce AI-related obligations, what penalties will apply, or how sectoral regulators will coordinate. FINMA covers financial services; the Federal Data Protection Commissioner covers data protection; healthcare regulators cover medical devices — but no horizontal AI supervisory function is proposed.

**No incident reporting.** No equivalent to the EU's Article 73 serious incident reporting or the tiered 2/10/15-day timelines.

### Feasibility

The approach is feasible precisely because it commits to very little. Sector-specific adjustments to existing law are lower-effort than a comprehensive Act. The end-of-2026 timeline for a FDJP bill is achievable for a focused legislative instrument implementing the Council of Europe Convention.

The deeper feasibility question is whether the approach is **sufficient** — not whether Switzerland can implement it, but whether it adequately addresses AI risks and EU market access requirements. Swiss life sciences and MedTech companies, for example, already need EU AI Act compliance for EU market access regardless of Swiss domestic law. Swiss financial institutions already operate under FINMA guidance. The domestic Swiss framework's practical impact may be limited to areas where Swiss-specific rules create obligations not already imposed by export-market regulation.

### Internal Consistency

Internally consistent. The three pillars (Convention, sector-specific, voluntary) form a coherent light-touch architecture. The explicit listing of "economic freedom" alongside fundamental rights is philosophically consistent with the innovation-friendly posture.

One tension: the Federal Council states it will "safeguard fundamental rights" including through transparency requirements and non-discrimination protections, but these protections depend on the FDJP bill (not yet drafted) and sector-specific adjustments (not yet specified). Fundamental-rights protection is stated as an objective but not operationalised.

### Coverage

Very limited at the announcement level. The decision establishes a direction (no AI Act; sector-specific + Convention) but does not itself contain regulatory provisions. Coverage will depend on the FDJP bill and the DETEC implementation plan, both due end of 2026.

### International Alignment

The Council of Europe AI Convention anchor is significant. The Convention has a broader signatory base than the EU AI Act and includes non-EU states (UK, US as observer, Israel, Japan, and others). Switzerland's alignment with the Convention rather than the AI Act places it in a multilateral framework that does not require EU regulatory convergence.

For UK-Swiss comparison, the approaches are philosophically close:
- Both reject comprehensive AI Acts
- Both prefer sector-specific / regulator-led approaches
- Both face EU market-access pressure
- Both have international convening roles (UK: Bletchley/Seoul/Paris summits + AISI; Switzerland: Geneva / ITU AI for Good)
- Key difference: UK has AISI (dedicated safety institute with 100+ researchers); Switzerland has no equivalent

For EU-Swiss comparison, the gap is structural:
- EU has binding horizontal regulation with risk tiers, prohibited practices, and GPAI provisions
- Switzerland has voluntary + sector-specific + Convention implementation
- Swiss firms serving the EU market comply with the EU AI Act directly; the Swiss framework is additive, not substitutive

---

## Overall Assessment

The February 2025 decision establishes Switzerland's **regulatory posture** rather than its **regulatory content**. It is a statement of philosophy (sector-specific, innovation-friendly, Convention-anchored) with a mandate for implementation (FDJP bill + DETEC plan by end 2026). The decision itself does not create obligations, define risk categories, or specify prohibited practices.

For UK-based firms advising Swiss clients or operating in Switzerland:

1. **Swiss domestic AI law will remain minimal through at least 2027.** The FDJP bill is due end of 2026; parliamentary process adds 1-2 years. Binding Swiss AI-specific obligations are unlikely before 2028.

2. **EU AI Act applies to Swiss firms serving the EU market.** The Swiss domestic framework does not substitute for AI Act compliance when exporting to the EU. Swiss firms in healthcare, MedTech, financial services, and other export-dependent sectors need parallel EU compliance.

3. **The Council of Europe Convention anchor is diplomatically significant but operationally light.** The Convention requires human-rights-compatible AI governance but does not prescribe specific operational requirements (no risk tiers, no prohibited practices, no GPAI thresholds).

4. **FINMA is the most operationally relevant Swiss AI regulator today.** Its Guidance 08/2024 (see [review 02](02-finma-guidance-ai-governance.md)) is the most substantive Swiss-specific AI compliance document. Financial sector firms should treat it as the primary domestic reference.

5. **Watch the FDJP bill (due end 2026).** This will determine whether Switzerland introduces transparency obligations, mandatory AI content labelling, political advertising rules, or enhanced data protection provisions that go beyond the current revFADP.
