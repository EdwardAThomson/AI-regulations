# AI Opportunities Action Plan — Review

**Document:** AI Opportunities Action Plan
**Author:** Matt Clifford CBE (commissioned by DSIT)
**Published:** January 2025
**Presented to Parliament by:** The Rt Hon Peter Kyle MP, Secretary of State for Science, Innovation and Technology
**Reference:** CP 1241

---

## Summary

The AI Opportunities Action Plan is the closest thing the UK has to a national AI strategy. Commissioned by the Secretary of State and authored by Matt Clifford, it sets out 50 recommendations across three pillars: laying the foundations (infrastructure, data, talent, regulation), driving adoption across public and private sectors, and building UK-headquartered frontier AI companies. The document's central thesis is that the UK must be "an AI maker, not just an AI taker" — it needs to build and shape AI, not merely consume it.

---

## Key Points

- 50 discrete recommendations organised under three sections: foundations (1–30), adoption (31–49), and homegrown AI (50)
- The government endorsed all 50 recommendations upon publication
- **Infrastructure:** Calls for a long-term compute plan backed by a 10-year investment commitment, 20x expansion of the AI Research Resource (AIRR) by 2030, AI Growth Zones for data centres, and sovereign/domestic/international compute tiers
- **Data:** Five high-impact public data sets to be rapidly identified; National Data Library to be developed; a copyright-cleared British media asset training data set to be created
- **Talent:** Tens of thousands of additional AI professionals to be trained by 2030; flagship AI scholarship programme; government headhunting capability to attract elite individuals; diversity interventions (only 22% of AI workforce are women)
- **Regulation:** Continue pro-innovation approach; grow AISI; reform text and data mining regime to be at least as competitive as the EU; fund regulator AI capabilities; require regulators to publish annual AI innovation reports
- **Adoption:** "Scan → Pilot → Scale" approach for government; AI lead for each government mission; AI Knowledge Hub; mission-focused national tenders
- **Homegrown AI:** Create a new unit, UK Sovereign AI, to partner with the private sector and maximise the UK's stake in frontier AI — explicitly compared to Japan's MITI and Singapore's Economic Development Board

---

## Observations

### Clarity

The document is well-structured and readable. Each recommendation is numbered and bolded, with explanatory context. The three-section architecture (foundations → adoption → homegrown) follows a logical progression from inputs to outputs to strategic positioning.

The "AI maker, not AI taker" framing is effective and memorable. Clifford is honest about the UK's starting position — third-largest AI market, genuinely world-leading in places, but at risk of falling behind the US and China.

However, clarity degrades at the recommendation level. Many use hedging language — "consider", "explore", "should seek to" — that makes it difficult to determine what the government is actually committing to. For example:

- Rec 21: "Explore how the existing immigration system *can be used* to attract graduates" — is this a commitment to change immigration policy, or a commitment to explore it?
- Rec 30: "Consider the broader institutional landscape and the full potential of the Alan Turing Institute" — consider doing what, exactly?
- Rec 47: "Leverage the new Industrial Strategy" — this is a statement of intent that depends entirely on a separate document that does not yet exist

The document frequently tells the government what it "should" do, but rarely specifies who is responsible for each action, by when, or with what resources. This is structurally a set of recommendations from an external adviser, not an implementation plan — a distinction that matters.

### Measurability

This is the document's weakest dimension. Of 50 recommendations, only a handful include anything resembling a measurable target:

- AIRR capacity to expand by "at least 20x by 2030" (Rec 2)
- "Tens of thousands" of additional AI professionals by 2030 (Sec 1.3)
- Internal AI role pay to "at least 75% of private-sector rate" (Rec 36)
- AI adoption could grow the UK economy by "an additional £400 billion by 2030" (Sec 2.4, but this is a projection, not a target)

The remaining 46+ recommendations have no baselines, no metrics, no timelines, and no success criteria. "Rapidly identify at least five high-impact public data sets" (Rec 7) is one of the more concrete commitments, but even this has no deadline.

The absence of measurability is not accidental — the document is explicitly a set of recommendations, not a delivery plan. But the government endorsed all 50 recommendations without adding measurable commitments, which means there is no basis on which to hold anyone accountable for delivery. The One Year On progress report (January 2026) attempts to fill this gap retroactively, but the original document provides no framework for measuring its own success.

### Gaps

**No budgets.** Not a single recommendation includes a cost estimate or funding commitment. The compute recommendations alone imply billions of pounds in investment, but the document does not say who pays. This is a strategy document that cannot be costed.

**Workers and labour market impact.** The foreword asserts that AI "opens up new opportunities rather than just threatens traditional patterns of work" — and that is essentially the extent of the document's engagement with the labour market disruption question. There is no analysis of displacement risk, no transition support proposal, and no engagement with the Fair Work agenda. Rec 19 acknowledges that "some jobs will be replaced by AI" but offers only lifelong skills programmes as a response.

**Devolution.** Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland are entirely absent. The document refers to "the UK" throughout but never addresses how the devolved governments' own AI strategies interact with UK-level policy. Given that Scotland published its own AI Strategy in March 2026 and has different approaches to Fair Work, public services, and economic development, this is a significant gap. The one mention of Scotland is a reference to AI Growth Zones potentially being sited in "post-industrial towns and coastal Scotland."

**Ethics and rights.** The document delegates ethics almost entirely to sector regulators and AISI. There is no discussion of citizens' rights in relation to AI, no mention of algorithmic transparency, no treatment of bias or discrimination beyond a passing reference to "marginalised groups," and no redress mechanisms. The Pro-Innovation White Paper covers some of this ground, but this document — the flagship strategy — treats ethics as an externality.

**Agentic AI.** The document mentions agentic systems once ("we will also very soon see agentic systems") but offers no governance framework, no risk analysis, and no policy response. Given that the document was published in January 2025, when agentic AI was already a major deployment trend, this is a forward-looking gap.

**Environmental sustainability.** The infrastructure section acknowledges that data centres need clean power, and Rec 5 mentions sustainability, but there is no quantitative treatment of the energy cost of the UK's compute ambitions. The document's framing is that AI infrastructure is a net benefit because it drives investment in renewables — but this claim is not evidenced.

**International governance.** The document is almost entirely domestic. There is no mention of the Bletchley Declaration (which the UK hosted), the OECD AI Principles, the Council of Europe AI Convention, or any multilateral governance framework. The only international reference is to the EuroHPC Joint Undertaking for compute partnerships. For a country that positioned itself as a leader in international AI governance through the Bletchley and Seoul summits, this is a conspicuous omission.

### Feasibility

The document's feasibility varies sharply by section.

**Infrastructure (strong).** The compute recommendations are ambitious but realistic. Private sector investment in UK data centres is already substantial, and the AI Growth Zones concept builds on existing planning reform momentum. The 20x AIRR expansion is a concrete, fundable commitment.

**Data (moderate).** The National Data Library concept is sound but faces the same challenges that have stalled previous UK data-sharing initiatives — fragmented ownership, privacy concerns, departmental resistance. The copyright-cleared British media training data set (Rec 13) is a genuinely novel idea with significant commercial potential, but depends on negotiations with the BBC, British Library, and others that could take years.

**Talent (challenging).** The UK faces intense global competition for top AI talent, particularly from the US. The document's talent recommendations are reasonable but conventional — scholarships, immigration reform, university expansion. The headhunting recommendation (Rec 20) is refreshingly honest about the difficulty of competing with private-sector compensation. Whether government can actually execute this at scale is uncertain.

**Regulation (uncertain).** The document endorses the pro-innovation approach while acknowledging it may not be sufficient. Rec 28 calls for regulators to publish annual innovation reports — a useful accountability mechanism. But the document also floats the idea of "a central body with a mandate and higher risk tolerance to promote innovation" that could issue sandbox licences overriding sector regulation. This is a radical proposal buried in a single paragraph, with no detail on how it would work.

**UK Sovereign AI (highly uncertain).** Recommendation 50 — the creation of a new unit to maximise the UK's stake in frontier AI — is the document's most ambitious and least detailed proposal. The MITI/Singapore EDB comparison is provocative but the document provides no information on governance, funding, mandate scope, or relationship to existing bodies like Innovate UK, UKRI, or the British Business Bank. This is a one-page sketch of an institution that would require years to design and build.

### Internal Consistency

There is a tension between the document's pro-innovation, light-touch regulatory stance and its recognition that regulatory clarity is needed. Section 1.4 simultaneously argues that the UK's "pro-innovation approach to regulation is a source of strength" and that "the UK is falling behind" on text and data mining reform. The document wants both less regulation and more regulation, depending on the topic.

The relationship between AISI and the broader regulatory framework is left unresolved. Rec 23 calls for continued growth of AISI, but the document does not address whether AISI should become a statutory regulator, how it relates to sector regulators' AI responsibilities, or how the UK's voluntary pre-deployment testing regime will evolve if companies stop cooperating.

Section 3's industrial activism — the government explicitly taking stakes in AI companies, backing national champions, using "all the levers of the state" — sits uneasily with the market-oriented, deregulatory tone of the rest of the document. The document acknowledges this tension ("a more activist approach and something more akin to Japan's MITI") but does not resolve it.

### Coverage

The document covers infrastructure, data, talent, regulation, public-sector adoption, private-sector adoption, and frontier AI development. Within these areas, coverage is reasonably thorough.

What it does not cover is notable: labour market disruption, ethical AI, citizens' rights, environmental impact, devolved government coordination, international governance, defence and security applications, and agentic AI governance. These are not minor topics — several of them are central to the AI strategies of comparator nations.

The document is fundamentally an economic growth strategy. It views AI almost exclusively through the lens of competitiveness, productivity, and national advantage. The social dimensions — who benefits, who loses, what safeguards exist — are treated as secondary concerns delegated to other instruments.

### International Alignment

The document positions the UK explicitly against the EU's regulatory approach ("at least as competitive as the EU" on IP, and a clear preference for lighter regulation). It draws favourable comparisons with Singapore and Japan on industrial strategy.

However, the document makes no reference to any international AI governance framework the UK has signed or endorsed. Given that the UK hosted the Bletchley AI Safety Summit in November 2023 — just 14 months before this document was published — and played a leading role in the Seoul and Paris summits, the complete absence of international governance commitments is striking. The document reads as though AI governance is a purely domestic concern.

---

## Overall Assessment

The AI Opportunities Action Plan is a clearly written, commercially literate strategy document that makes a compelling case for the UK to invest aggressively in AI infrastructure, talent, and industrial capacity. Its strongest sections — on compute, data, and the "Scan → Pilot → Scale" approach to government adoption — contain practical, actionable recommendations.

Its fundamental weakness is that it is an economic strategy pretending to be a comprehensive AI plan. By treating workers, citizens, ethics, the environment, and international governance as externalities delegated to other documents, it creates a strategy that is coherent on its own terms but incomplete as a national AI position. The absence of measurable targets, budgets, timelines, and accountability mechanisms means that the government can claim to have endorsed all 50 recommendations without being held to any specific delivery standard.

The document's most revealing moment is Recommendation 50 — the creation of UK Sovereign AI. This is either the most important AI policy proposal in the UK's recent history or a one-page thought experiment. The fact that it is impossible to tell which, from the text alone, is indicative of the document's approach throughout: bold in aspiration, thin on commitment.
