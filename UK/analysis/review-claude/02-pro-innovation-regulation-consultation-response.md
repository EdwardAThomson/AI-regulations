# A Pro-Innovation Approach to AI Regulation: Government Response — Review

**Document:** A pro-innovation approach to AI regulation: government response to consultation
**Published:** February 2024
**Issuing body:** Department for Science, Innovation and Technology (DSIT)
**Presented to Parliament by:** The Rt Hon Michelle Donelan MP, Secretary of State for Science, Innovation and Technology
**Reference:** CP 1019

**Note:** The source PDF in this repository is filed as `Pro-Innovation-Approach-AI-Regulation-White-Paper-Mar-2023.pdf`, but the content is the February 2024 government response, not the original March 2023 white paper. The original white paper proposed the framework; this document reports on the 12-week public consultation (409 written responses, 364 roundtable participants) and sets out the government's evolved position.

---

## Summary

This is the UK government's formal response to its consultation on AI regulation. It reaffirms the five cross-sectoral principles (safety, transparency, fairness, accountability, contestability) and the context-based, sector-regulator-led approach proposed in the original white paper, while making a significant policy evolution: the document now explicitly argues that binding measures will be needed for developers of highly capable general-purpose AI systems. It announces over 100 million pounds for AI innovation and regulation, 10 million pounds for regulator capabilities, a new central function within DSIT with eight workstreams, the AI Safety Institute's evaluation programme, and the DRCF's AI and Digital Hub pilot. The document also summarises detailed consultation evidence across 33 questions and four annexes.

---

## Key Points

- **Five principles reaffirmed** on a non-statutory basis: safety/security/robustness, transparency/explainability, fairness, accountability/governance, contestability/redress. Government keeps statutory duty under review but does not commit to it
- **Central function established** within DSIT across eight workstreams: risk assessment, regulator capabilities, regulator powers review, coordination (new steering committee by spring 2024), research/innovation, ease of compliance (DRCF AI and Digital Hub), public trust, monitoring/evaluation
- **10 million pounds** announced for regulator AI capabilities; UKRI commits to linking AI research investments to regulator skills
- **Regulators asked to publish strategic AI updates** by 30 April 2024, including risk analyses and 12-month forward plans. Letters sent to 14+ regulators including Ofcom, ICO, FCA, CMA, EHRC, MHRA, Ofsted, HSE, Bank of England, and others
- **Binding measures for frontier AI**: The document makes the "pro-innovation case" for targeted mandatory requirements on the "small number of organisations developing highly capable general-purpose AI systems." It proposes dynamic thresholds based on compute and capability benchmarking, and outlines potential obligations including transparency on training data, risk management, corporate governance, and pre-deployment testing
- **AI Safety Institute** formally established with three core functions: evaluations of advanced AI systems, foundational safety research, and information exchange. Leading AI companies pledged priority access to their systems for testing. AISI partnerships with US and Singapore announced
- **DRCF AI and Digital Hub** launched as a pilot multi-regulator advisory service (CMA, ICO, Ofcom, FCA) to help innovators navigate overlapping regulatory regimes
- **Copyright impasse acknowledged**: the IPO's working group of rights holders and AI developers "could not agree an effective voluntary code." DSIT and DCMS ministers to lead further engagement
- **Open source**: Government acknowledges value but states all "powerful and potentially dangerous systems should be thoroughly risk-assessed before being released," signalling that open release may face constraints
- **DSIT AI team scaled** from approximately 20 people (Feb 2023) to over 160 (end 2023), with plans for 270+ in 2024. Office for AI branding retired; CDEI renamed to Responsible Technology Adoption Unit
- **Consultation findings**: Over half of respondents said existing redress routes are inadequate; a majority agreed the principles would cover AI risks but flagged enforcement concerns; half felt compute was an inadequate governance proxy; respondents were strongly supportive of regulatory sandboxes, particularly multi-sector/multi-regulator models
- **Bletchley Declaration** (November 2023): 28 countries plus the EU agreed on frontier AI risk-sharing and safety collaboration. Document references extensive multilateral engagement through G7, G20, GPAI, OECD, Council of Europe, UNESCO, and bilateral partnerships
- **Algorithmic Transparency Recording Standard** made mandatory for all government departments, with plans to expand across the broader public sector

## Observations

### Clarity

The document is well-structured and clearly written for a government consultation response. The policy architecture is legible: five principles, sector-regulator implementation, central function for coordination, AISI for frontier safety, and the emerging case for binding measures on the most capable systems.

However, two significant clarity problems undermine the document:

First, the relationship between the non-statutory principles and the proposed binding measures is left deliberately ambiguous. The document simultaneously argues that the non-statutory approach "offers critical adaptability" and that "mandatory measures will ultimately be required across all jurisdictions." It states the government will "legislate when we are confident that it is the right thing to do" but provides no criteria for when that confidence threshold would be met. This creates a policy framework where every position is defensible because no position is committed to.

Second, the document conflates its role as a consultation response with policy announcement. The eight central function workstreams, the 10 million pound funding package, and the AISI establishment are presented alongside summaries of what consultation respondents said, making it difficult to distinguish between what the government is doing, what it is considering, and what respondents suggested. The numbered paragraph structure helps, but the overall effect is a document that reports extensively on views received without always being clear about what has changed as a result.

The glossary (pp. 9-10) is a genuinely useful contribution, particularly the three-way distinction between highly capable general-purpose AI, highly capable narrow AI, and agentic AI. This taxonomic clarity is more precise than most national strategy documents achieve.

### Measurability

The document's measurability is weak on outcomes but reasonable on process commitments. Process commitments with timelines include:

- Regulator strategic updates by 30 April 2024
- New steering committee for AI governance by spring 2024
- Updated guidance on AI in HR and recruitment in spring 2024
- Targeted consultation on cross-economy AI risk register in 2024
- Monitoring and evaluation plan consultation in spring 2024
- AISI evaluation approach publication (imminent at time of writing)
- Updated emerging safety processes guide by end of 2024
- BRAID programme Phase 2 extending to 2027/28

Financial commitments are specified: 10 million pounds for regulator capabilities, 2 million plus 7.6 million for BRAID, 9 million for US partnership on responsible AI, 80 million for AI research hubs, 1.5 billion for public sector supercomputers.

What is entirely absent is any framework for measuring whether the regulatory approach is working. The document commits to developing a monitoring and evaluation plan but does not propose any outcome metrics — no targets for regulator readiness, no benchmarks for public trust, no measures of harm reduction, no assessment criteria for whether the non-statutory approach is sufficient or needs to become statutory. The consultation responses themselves suggested multiple concrete KPIs (consumer trust and satisfaction, rate of innovation, time to market, complaints and adverse events, compliance costs), but the government's response is to promise a future consultation on metrics rather than to adopt any.

### Gaps

**No timeline for legislation.** The document's most significant policy development — the case for binding measures on frontier AI developers — has no timeline, no legislative vehicle identified, and no trigger conditions specified. The ten "key questions for policy development" in Box 5 are genuinely important questions, but they amount to a research agenda, not a legislative programme. Given that the EU AI Act was already finalised when this document was published, the absence of any legislative timetable is a deliberate strategic choice — but it leaves the UK's regulatory position dependent on voluntary compliance by frontier AI companies.

**Devolution remains unaddressed.** The document acknowledges in passing that "some [sectors] are reserved and some of which are devolved" and commits to "engaging the territorial offices and devolved administrations." But there is no analysis of which regulatory powers are devolved, no mention of the Scottish Government's or Welsh Government's AI positions, and no mechanism for resolving potential divergence between UK and devolved approaches.

**Labour market impact treated superficially.** The document cites DfE work on AI impact on UK jobs and 3.8 billion pounds in skills investment, but its response to the displacement question is limited to existing programmes (apprenticeships, Skills Bootcamps, Lifelong Learning Entitlement). There is no analysis of which sectors face the highest automation risk, no workforce transition plan, and no engagement with unions beyond mentioning "trade unions" as a respondent category.

**Consumer redress.** Over half of consultation respondents said existing redress routes for AI-related harms are inadequate. Civil society participants "strongly supported a central ombudsman for AI." The government's response is to acknowledge the issue but propose nothing concrete — no ombudsman, no new redress mechanism, no timeline for addressing the gap.

**Environmental impact.** Not addressed. The document discusses data centre investment, compute expansion, and AI infrastructure, but contains no analysis of the energy or environmental implications.

**Copyright resolution deferred.** The document honestly acknowledges the IPO working group's failure to agree a voluntary code, but the proposed next step — ministers leading "a period of engagement" — is the weakest possible response to a demonstrated failure of voluntary coordination. No timeline, no fallback mechanism, no indication of what happens if engagement also fails.

### Feasibility

The feasibility of the framework depends on two things working simultaneously: sector regulators effectively implementing the principles within their existing remits, and the central function successfully coordinating across 14+ regulators with varying capabilities.

**Regulator readiness is uneven.** The document itself acknowledges that regulators reported "varying levels of in-house AI knowledge and capability" and that some "struggle to compete with the private sector for the right technical expertise and skills." Two regulators explicitly told the consultation that the scope of their existing powers in relation to AI is unclear. The 10 million pound capability fund is a start, but it is spread across 14+ regulators — less than 700,000 each on average, which is insufficient to build meaningful technical capacity.

**AISI is well-designed but under-mandated.** The AI Safety Institute's three-function model (evaluations, research, information exchange) is coherent, and the document is refreshingly honest that AISI's role is "not to designate any particular AI system as 'safe.'" But AISI operates entirely on voluntary cooperation from frontier AI companies. The document acknowledges that "commercial incentives are not always aligned with the public good" and that if more firms build highly capable systems, "the governance of voluntary approaches will be much harder." The gap between this diagnosis and the proposed intervention (continued voluntary cooperation) is the document's central feasibility risk.

**The central function is ambitious for its resources.** Eight workstreams covering risk assessment, regulator coordination, research, compliance support, public trust, and monitoring — delivered by a team that grew from 20 to 160 in one year — is a significant organisational challenge. The document plans for 270+ staff in 2024, but does not address whether DSIT can recruit and retain the necessary technical expertise in a competitive market.

### Internal Consistency

The document contains several unresolved tensions:

**Voluntary vs. mandatory.** The document simultaneously maintains that voluntary measures are working and that binding measures will be needed. It argues that "the intense competition between companies to release ever-more-capable systems means the government will need to remain highly vigilant to meaningful compliance" — an argument that voluntary measures are structurally inadequate — while continuing to rely on them. The proposed resolution is temporal (voluntary now, mandatory later), but the criteria for transition are undefined.

**Context-based vs. entity-based regulation.** The core framework is context-based — regulate the use, not the technology. But Section 5.2 builds the case for entity-based regulation of frontier AI developers, because the context-based approach "may not adequately address risks arising from highly capable general-purpose systems." The document does not explain how these two approaches will coexist. Will frontier AI developers face both entity-level obligations and context-level regulation through sector regulators? How will overlaps be managed?

**Pro-innovation vs. precautionary.** The document's title is "pro-innovation" but its most substantive policy development is the case for pre-deployment testing and potential restrictions on open release. The statement that "all powerful and potentially dangerous systems should be thoroughly risk-assessed before being released" is a precautionary principle in all but name. The document does not acknowledge this tension.

**Central function vs. regulator independence.** Regulators told the consultation they valued the central function but stressed it "should not infringe on the independence or existing statutory remits of regulators." The document agrees — but the central function's workstreams include reviewing regulator powers, coordinating regulator approaches, and monitoring framework effectiveness. These are inherently supervisory activities. How the central function will coordinate without directing is left unresolved.

### Coverage

The document's coverage is broader than the AI Opportunities Action Plan, reflecting its regulatory focus. It addresses:

- Safety, security, and robustness (including cyber security)
- Transparency and explainability
- Fairness, bias, and discrimination
- Accountability and governance (including life cycle accountability)
- Contestability and redress
- Foundation models and the AI supply chain
- IP and copyright
- Electoral integrity
- Data protection (via DPDI Bill)
- Competition (via CMA and DMCC Bill)
- Public sector AI use and procurement
- Misuse risks (biosecurity, crime, disinformation)
- Autonomy risks (agentic AI, alignment)
- International governance

This is genuinely comprehensive. The areas it treats superficially — labour markets, environmental impact, devolution, consumer redress — are notable precisely because the rest of the coverage is thorough.

### International Alignment

This is the document's strongest section. The international engagement is genuinely extensive: Bletchley Declaration, Seoul and Paris summit commitments, G7 Hiroshima AI Process, G20 AI Principles, GPAI, OECD AI Principles, Council of Europe Framework Convention, UNESCO AI Ethics Recommendation, US-UK partnership on responsible AI, bilateral AISI partnerships with US and Singapore, and the International Scientific Report on AI Safety chaired by Yoshua Bengio.

The UK's positioning is clear: lead on safety through AISI and summits, maintain lighter regulation than the EU to attract investment, and build bilateral safety-testing partnerships rather than submitting to multilateral regulatory frameworks. This is a coherent strategy, though it depends on the UK's convening power surviving changes of government and the continued willingness of frontier AI companies to cooperate voluntarily with a country that has not yet legislated.

The document is notably silent on the EU AI Act, which had been finalised by the time of publication. Given that the EU AI Act directly regulates the same frontier AI developers the UK is proposing voluntary measures for, this omission seems deliberate — the government does not want to draw attention to the contrast between its voluntary approach and the EU's binding one.

---

## Overall Assessment

This consultation response is a thorough, honest, and strategically important document. It represents a genuine evolution from the original March 2023 white paper: where the white paper proposed principles and a framework, this response confronts the limitations of that framework — particularly for frontier AI — and begins building the case for binding measures.

The document's greatest strength is its honesty about the challenges. It acknowledges that the voluntary approach may be insufficient, that regulators lack capability, that existing legal frameworks do not reach frontier AI developers, that the copyright working group failed, and that commercial incentives may not align with public safety. This diagnostic honesty is unusual in government publications and provides a solid foundation for future policy development.

Its greatest weakness is the gap between diagnosis and action. The document identifies multiple problems — inadequate regulator capability, insufficient developer accountability, absent consumer redress, unresolved copyright — and responds to each with further consultation, engagement, or research. The ten "key questions" in Box 5 are important, but they read as a research agenda that could take years to resolve. Meanwhile, frontier AI capabilities continue to advance, the EU AI Act creates binding obligations, and the UK's position depends on voluntary cooperation from companies facing no legal consequences for withdrawing it.

The consultation evidence itself is remarkably consistent in its messages: respondents want statutory backing for the principles, want clear liability allocation, want consumer redress mechanisms, want regulators to be properly resourced, and are sceptical that compute is an adequate governance proxy. The government's response to these findings is to acknowledge them sympathetically while committing to very little.

The document's most consequential policy position is also its least developed: the case for binding measures on frontier AI developers, with dynamic thresholds based on compute and capability benchmarking. If this becomes legislation, it will define the UK's approach to AI governance for a generation. But the document provides no timeline, no legislative vehicle, no institutional design, and no enforcement mechanism. It is a promissory note, not a policy.
