# AI Opportunities Action Plan: One Year On — Review

**Document:** AI Opportunities Action Plan: One Year On — Progress Update
**Published:** January 2026
**Issuing body:** Department for Science, Innovation and Technology (DSIT)
**Forewords by:** The Rt Hon Keir Starmer KCB KC MP, Prime Minister; The Rt Hon Liz Kendall MP, Secretary of State for Science, Innovation and Technology

---

## Summary

This is a 15-page progress report on the AI Opportunities Action Plan, published one year after the original plan's 50 recommendations were endorsed. The headline claim is that 38 of 50 actions have been met. The document is structured around the same three pillars as the original plan — lay the foundations, change lives by embracing AI, and secure the future with homegrown AI — and reports tangible delivery against each. Notable developments include: five AI Growth Zones designated, Isambard-AI launched at Bristol, over 100 million pounds for the National Data Library, 240 million pounds for AISI, the Sovereign AI Unit established with up to 500 million pounds, 1 million free AI upskilling courses delivered, and the creation of a new Future of Work unit. The document also references a public dashboard for tracking progress.

---

## Key Points

### Foundations

**Compute and Infrastructure:**
- Five AI Growth Zones designated across Great Britain (2 in Wales, 1 in Scotland, others in England), generating 28.2 billion pounds in investment and creating more than 15,000 jobs. 5 million pounds targeted funding per zone. AI Growth Zone Delivery Unit established to broker power, planning, and offtake
- Isambard-AI supercomputer launched at Bristol University (July 2025). Up to 250 million pounds earmarked to scale up cloud capacity for AIRR
- Cambridge DAWN supercomputer capacity to increase sixfold by spring 2026
- Next national supercomputer backed by 750 million pounds government funding, to be located at the Edinburgh Parallel Computing Centre linked to a new "British Library for the AI age" data facility at Isambard-AI
- Compute positioned near renewable power: Small Modular Reactors in North Wales, a micro-grid in Lanarkshire
- DSIT and AISI/NCSC established new research programme on secure AI computing systems

**Data:**
- National Data Library backed with over 100 million pounds at Spending Review 2025. Published guidelines for making government datasets AI-ready. Launched first open calls for data proposals
- Health Data Research Service launched: government and Wellcome committed up to 600 million pounds for a secure, single access point to national health datasets. CEO appointed January 2026
- AI Education Content Store: curated curriculum and assessment corpora to make AI more accurate for teachers
- Creative Content Exchange: pilot phase with 12 leading cultural institutions (National Archives, Natural History Museum, Royal Armouries) to license digitised cultural and creative assets at scale
- Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 enacted: new powers for Smart Data schemes. 36 million pounds investment in new schemes

**Talent:**
- Over 1 million free AI courses delivered through industry partners. AI Skills Boost platform launched. Target raised to upskilling 10 million workers by 2030
- 187 million pounds committed to TechFirst (flagship tech skills programme)
- 7.8 million pounds for TechLocal: AI Professional Degree and Traineeship Accelerator
- TechGrad undergraduate scholarships launched: 350 students for first cohort (2026), scaling to 900/year from 2026/27
- Sparck AI scholarship: full tuition and stipends for master's students across 9 universities
- Dedicated AI sourcing function in government: operating at the level of top-tier search firms, staffed by operators from X, Google DeepMind. AISI scaled to over 100 technical staff with alumni from OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Anthropic, Meta, Cohere, Palantir
- Global Talent Taskforce resourced doubled (January 2026): new functions to support relocation, visa fee reimbursements, expanded High Potential Individual/Innovator Founder/Global Talent immigration routes
- **New: Future of Work programme** — cross-government unit backed by a panel of experts from business and trade unions. Will provide analysis and evidence on AI's labour market impact and advise on policy

### Regulation, Safety, and Assurance

- AISI backed with 240 million pounds at Spending Review 2025. Now has over 100 researchers, has tested 30 frontier models, produced a systematic evidence base. Published Frontier AI Trends Report, 10 peer-reviewed papers at NeurIPS 2025, a landmark study on AI-driven persuasion in *Science*, and an international Alignment Project
- AISI chairs the International Network for AI Measurement, Evaluation and Science — shaping global standards for evaluating advanced systems
- Trusted Third-Party AI Assurance Roadmap published. 11 million pound AI Assurance Innovation Fund created. New Centre for AI Measurement at the National Physical Laboratory
- AI Assurance Innovation Fund opens for applications spring 2026
- AI Growth Lab: cross-economy sandbox for responsible AI deployment (call for evidence launched). MHRA Airlock phase two launched (clinical AI assistants for doctors). FCA "Supercharged Sandbox" launched with Nvidia
- Additional regulator funding: 3.6 million AI Capability Fund + 8.9 million Regulators' Pioneer Fund
- Regulators to publish plans for enabling safe AI-powered innovation and report annually

### Public Sector Adoption

- Minute: AI-powered scribe for government meetings, scaled to 1,000 officials in 22 local authorities. Being developed as a live service by MHCLG's Local AI programme
- Extract: AI tool for processing planning documents, expected available to all councils by spring 2026
- AI tool for planning decisions: aiming to cut processing time by 50%, with longer-term ambition of near-instant decisions. Regional trials by May 2026, national scale-up by 2027
- AI tutoring tools: tenders by March 2026 for co-creation and in-school trials. Could support up to 450,000 children on free school meals in years 9-11
- NHS AI Diagnostic Fund: one-third of NHS chest X-rays (2.4 million scans) now AI-assisted
- AI Accelerator Tenders: regional-scale products in 6 months
- AI scan function in the Incubator for AI (i.AI): single front door for procurement
- AI Commercial Strategy developed (January 2026): prioritises buying from market and challenge-led procurement

### Private Sector Adoption

- Modern Industrial Strategy launched June 2025: AI across 8 priority sectors, backed by 8 AI Sector Champions
- 150 million pounds for 6 transformative AI and technology programmes
- 40 million pounds for Robotics Adoption Hubs and Made Smarter expansion
- Innovate UK's BridgeAI programme expanded across priority sectors

### Homegrown AI

- Sovereign AI Unit established. First-year activities: allocated sovereign compute to researchers and start-ups (e.g. Cambridge MACE materials foundation model), invested in data assets (8 million seed for OpenBind consortium structural dataset for drug discovery), scaled Pillar VC and ARIA's Encode Fellowship
- Next phase of Sovereign AI Unit launches April 2026: backed by up to 500 million pounds, chaired by James Wise (Balderton Capital), delivered by DSIT. Will invest in and support UK AI companies in critical parts of the value chain

## Observations

### Clarity

The document is clearly written and well-structured, matching the three-pillar architecture of the original plan. Each section leads with a bold claim and provides specific supporting evidence. The writing is more concrete than the original plan — where the plan said "consider" and "explore," this document says "launched," "committed," and "delivered."

However, the document's format is selective rather than comprehensive. It reports on highlights rather than systematically tracking all 50 recommendations. The reference to a "public dashboard" for detailed tracking is helpful, but the document itself does not provide a recommendation-by-recommendation status update. The claim of "38 of 50 actions met" cannot be verified from the document alone — there is no annex listing which 38 are complete and which 12 remain outstanding. This is a significant transparency gap for a progress report.

### Measurability

This is a substantial improvement over the original plan. Where the plan was almost entirely unmeasured, this document provides numerous concrete figures:

- 28.2 billion pounds in AI Growth Zone investment
- 15,000+ jobs from Growth Zones
- 240 million pounds for AISI (Spending Review 2025)
- 100 million+ for National Data Library
- 600 million pounds for Health Data Research Service
- 750 million pounds for next national supercomputer
- 500 million pounds for Sovereign AI Unit
- 187 million pounds for TechFirst
- 1 million AI courses delivered
- 30 frontier models tested by AISI
- 100+ researchers at AISI
- 2.4 million NHS chest X-rays AI-assisted (one-third of total)
- 1,000 officials using Minute AI scribe across 22 local authorities

However, these are almost all input/output metrics (money committed, things launched, courses delivered). There are very few outcome metrics: Is AI adoption actually increasing? Are public services measurably improving? Is the UK's competitive position strengthening or weakening relative to comparators? The 10 million workers upskilling target by 2030 is a useful outcome measure, but it is the exception. The document reports what the government has done, not what has changed as a result.

### Gaps

**The 12 unmet actions.** The most important missing information is which 12 of 50 recommendations have not been met and why. Without this, the 38/50 claim is unverifiable and the document reads as a curated selection of successes. A one-page annex mapping all 50 recommendations to their status would have transformed the document's credibility.

**Copyright and IP.** The original plan's recommendation on a copyright-cleared British media training data set is not mentioned. The Creative Content Exchange is referenced, but it is a licensing marketplace — not a resolution of the copyright impasse that the February 2024 consultation response acknowledged. Given that the IPO working group failed and ministers promised further engagement, the silence on copyright outcomes is conspicuous.

**Regulation.** The original plan endorsed the pro-innovation approach and called for regulators to publish annual AI innovation reports. This progress report mentions regulator funding (3.6 million + 8.9 million) and states regulators "will publish a plan" and "report on this annually," but does not report on whether regulators have actually done so over the past year. The broader question of whether the non-statutory approach is working — the central question of the regulatory framework — is not addressed.

**Workers and displacement.** The creation of the Future of Work unit is a genuinely significant new development that addresses the most important gap in the original plan. However, the document provides no evidence of what the unit has found or recommended. It is presented as a structural response (we created a unit) rather than a substantive one (here is what we learned about AI's labour market impact). The original plan's near-total silence on displacement risk is partially addressed by the unit's existence but not by any analysis or policy response.

**Devolution.** One of the five AI Growth Zones is in Scotland and two are in Wales. This is the only engagement with devolution in the document. There is no mention of coordination with devolved governments on AI strategy, skills, or public service deployment.

**International governance.** The original plan was criticised (in our review) for being almost entirely domestic. This progress report is also entirely domestic. AISI's international role is mentioned (chairing the International Network for AI Measurement, Evaluation and Science), but there is no reference to the Bletchley/Seoul/Paris summit process, OECD, GPAI, or any multilateral governance framework. For a country that claims global AI governance leadership, the complete absence of international engagement from a flagship progress report is notable.

**Environmental sustainability.** The document notes that Growth Zones are "positioned near renewable power" (Small Modular Reactors, micro-grids), but provides no data on the energy cost of the UK's compute expansion. The 28.2 billion pounds in data centre investment implies substantial energy demand, but this is not quantified or addressed.

**Ethics and citizens' rights.** Not mentioned. The AI Assurance ecosystem work (NPL Centre, Assurance Innovation Fund, Third-Party Assurance Roadmap) addresses safety but not fairness, bias, or citizens' rights.

### Feasibility

The document demonstrates that the most feasible elements of the original plan — infrastructure investment, compute procurement, skills programmes, and public sector pilots — have been delivered. The Growth Zones, Isambard-AI, and the NHS diagnostic fund are real, funded, and operational.

The more challenging elements remain works in progress. The Sovereign AI Unit has been established but its substantive investment phase (up to 500 million) launches in April 2026 — it has not yet proven it can identify and back UK AI companies at scale. The AI tutoring trials are promising but are still at the tender stage. The planning AI tool targets of 50% processing time reduction and "near-instant decisions" are ambitious claims that are not yet evidenced.

The most uncertain element is whether the institutional innovations — the Future of Work unit, the AI Growth Lab sandbox, the AI Assurance ecosystem — will produce meaningful outcomes or remain structural commitments without substantive impact. The document establishes their existence but cannot yet demonstrate their value.

### Internal Consistency

The document is consistent with the original plan's architecture and priorities. It follows the same three-section structure and reports against the same themes. The shift from "recommendations" to "delivery" language is appropriate for a progress report.

One notable evolution: the document introduces the Future of Work programme and explicitly acknowledges that "AI will reshape the world of work" and the need to "protect communities from the mistakes of past industrial change." This is a significant tonal shift from the original plan, which treated labour market impact as a secondary concern. The progress report elevates it to a named programme with ministerial backing. Whether this reflects genuine policy change or rhetorical adjustment will depend on what the unit actually produces.

The document also reveals an implicit tension between speed and safety. The forewords emphasise "delivery at pace" and "going further and faster," while the regulation section describes building safety infrastructure (AISI, assurance, sandboxes). These are not contradictory, but the balance of emphasis — far more space devoted to acceleration than to safeguarding — reveals the government's priorities.

### Coverage

For a 15-page progress report, the coverage is reasonable. It touches on compute, data, talent, regulation, safety, public sector adoption, private sector adoption, and industrial strategy. The sections are unequally weighted — foundations (infrastructure, data, talent, regulation) receive 8 pages, adoption receives 3 pages, and homegrown AI receives 1 page.

Coverage gaps relative to the original plan: copyright/IP, international governance, devolution, environmental sustainability, citizens' rights, and detailed recommendation tracking. Several of these are the same gaps identified in the original plan — they have not been addressed in the intervening year.

---

## Overall Assessment

This progress report is a competent piece of government communication that demonstrates genuine delivery against many of the original plan's recommendations. The infrastructure investments (Growth Zones, Isambard-AI, DAWN expansion, Edinburgh supercomputer), data initiatives (National Data Library, Health Data Research Service), and skills programmes (1 million courses, TechFirst, Sparck, TechGrad) are substantial and concrete. The NHS diagnostic deployment (2.4 million AI-assisted chest X-rays) and local government tools (Minute, Extract) show real public service impact. The creation of the Future of Work unit addresses the original plan's most important gap.

The document's fundamental weakness is that it is a highlight reel, not an accountability report. The 38/50 claim is unverifiable from the text. The 12 unmet actions are not identified. There are no outcome metrics — no evidence that AI adoption has increased, public services have improved, or the UK's competitive position has strengthened. The financial commitments are large (easily 2+ billion pounds in aggregate), but there is no framework for assessing whether this investment is delivering value.

The most telling omission is regulation. The original plan endorsed 50 recommendations, many of which touched on regulatory reform. One year later, the regulatory framework remains non-statutory, the statutory duty on regulators has not been introduced, the copyright impasse remains unresolved, and the binding measures for frontier AI developers proposed in the 2024 consultation response have not materialised. The progress report does not acknowledge or address any of these outstanding regulatory questions. AISI's growth (100+ researchers, 30 models tested, 240 million pounds) is the one clear regulatory-adjacent success — but AISI is a safety research institute, not a regulator, and its work remains voluntary.

The document succeeds as a demonstration that the government is spending money and launching programmes. It does not succeed as evidence that the AI Opportunities Action Plan is achieving its stated objectives of making the UK "an AI maker, not just an AI taker."
