# AI Opportunities Action Plan: Government Response — Review

**Document:** AI Opportunities Action Plan Government Response
**Published:** January 2025
**Issuing body:** Department for Science, Innovation and Technology (DSIT)
**Presented to Parliament by:** The Secretary of State for Science, Innovation and Technology
**Reference:** CP 1242

**Note:** Published alongside the AI Opportunities Action Plan itself, this is the government's formal, recommendation-by-recommendation response. It provides a tabular format showing each of the 50 recommendations, the government's response (Agree / Partially Agree), and a delivery timeline. This is the only document in the UK's AI governance landscape that systematically maps commitments to timelines.

---

## Summary

This 20-page document is the government's formal endorsement of the AI Opportunities Action Plan. Published simultaneously with the Clifford plan, it provides a recommendation-by-recommendation response to all 50 recommendations, organised under the plan's three-pillar structure (foundations, adoption, homegrown AI) plus detailed section-by-section tables. The government agrees with 47 recommendations and partially agrees with 3 (Recs 13, 21, 49). Each response includes a delivery timeline, most falling in spring or summer 2025 or autumn 2025-2026. The most significant policy commitment is the intention to establish AISI as a statutory body and consult on proposed legislation to provide regulatory certainty for AI. The document also confirms the creation of AI Growth Zones, a new sovereign AI function, the National Data Library, an AI Energy Council, and a skills assessment through the Industrial Strategy Council.

---

## Key Points

### Response Pattern

Of 50 recommendations:
- **47 Agree** — full endorsement with delivery plan
- **3 Partially Agree** — Recs 13 (copyright-cleared media training data set), 21 (immigration for AI talent), and 49 (private sector AI adoption targets)
- **0 Disagree** — no recommendation rejected

The three "Partially Agree" responses are revealing:
- **Rec 13** (copyright-cleared British media training data set): "DCMS and DSIT will engage with partner organisations and industry to consider the potential role of government in taking forward this recommendation." This is the weakest response in the document — a commitment to consider, not to act.
- **Rec 21** (use immigration system to attract top AI graduates): The government points to existing visa routes (HPI, Skilled Worker, Innovator Founder) rather than committing to new immigration reform.
- **Rec 49** (set ambitious adoption targets for private sector): Response deferred to the Industrial Strategy.

### Narrative Sections (pp. 5-7)

Before the tables, the document provides brief narrative sections for each pillar:

**Lay the foundations:**
- Commits to expanding sovereign compute capacity by at least 20x by 2030
- New supercomputing facility to double AIRR capacity — DSIT to develop business case and lock in site/suppliers in 2025
- AIRR accessible to researchers and SMEs from early 2025 via Isambard-AI (Bristol) and Dawn (Cambridge)
- Archer2 (Edinburgh) extended until November 2026
- Sovereign compute investment complements 25+ billion in private data centre investment since July 2024
- Long-term compute strategy to be published spring 2025
- First AI Growth Zone at Culham (UKAEA HQ), subject to public-private partnership agreement
- AI Energy Council co-chaired by Science/Technology and Energy Secretaries
- National Data Library to be created with privacy-preserving safeguards
- Skills England and Curriculum and Assessment Review to address AI skills
- Regulatory approach and competitive copyright regime to be set out

**Change lives by embracing AI:**
- "Scan, Pilot, Scale" approach to be adopted for AI in public services
- AI Champions to be appointed in sectors identified in the Industrial Strategy
- DSIT to work with devolved and local government to identify AI adoption opportunities

**Secure our future with homegrown AI:**
- New sovereign AI function to partner with AI companies, with mandate to:
  - Leverage Growth Zones and compute for partnered companies
  - Make high-potential data sets available via the National Data Library
  - Support top AI talent relocation to work with UK-based companies
  - Build relationships between partnered companies and the national security community
- Further details by spring 2025

**Next steps:**
- AI Opportunities Unit created within DSIT to track delivery
- Policy response to continue ahead of spring 2025 Spending Review
- Wider approach to AI to be set out in the Industrial Strategy's Digital and Technologies Sector Plan

### Regulation and Safety (Rec 23 — Most Significant)

The response to Recommendation 23 is the document's most consequential commitment: "DSIT will confirm AISI's funding through upcoming Spending Reviews. DSIT will consult on proposed legislation to provide regulatory certainty to help kickstart growth and protect UK citizens and assets from the critical risks associated with the next generation of the most powerful AI models. **The government intends to establish AISI as a statutory body.**"

This is the first explicit commitment to AI-specific legislation in the UK's governance framework. The pro-innovation white paper and consultation response both deferred legislation; this document commits to it, specifically for AISI's statutory footing and regulatory certainty for frontier AI.

## Observations

### Clarity

The tabular format is the document's greatest strength. Each recommendation is presented alongside its response and delivery timeline, making it possible to track commitments systematically. This is a significant improvement over the narrative-only format of the original plan and the consultation response.

However, the quality of responses varies enormously. Some are specific and actionable — Rec 4 (Growth Zones) identifies Culham as the first site and describes the public-private partnership model. Others are circular — Rec 43 ("Procure smartly from the AI ecosystem") receives the response "DSIT will procure smartly from the AI ecosystem." Many data-related recommendations (Recs 7-12) receive identical responses ("Agree. See above."), directing the reader to a single paragraph about the National Data Library. This bundles six distinct recommendations into a single undifferentiated commitment.

The narrative sections (pp. 5-7) are clear but thin — they provide political framing rather than policy detail. The substantive content is in the tables.

### Measurability

The delivery timelines are the document's most important contribution to the UK's AI governance landscape. For the first time, each of the 50 recommendations has a target date. This creates an accountability framework that the original plan entirely lacked.

However, the timelines are process-oriented rather than outcome-oriented. "Spring 2025" for the compute strategy means a document will be published, not that compute capacity will have expanded. "Summer 2025" for the National Data Library means further details will be provided, not that the library will be operational. The only timeline that implies delivery of an actual capability is Rec 4 (first Growth Zone at Culham, spring 2025).

Many timelines use "Update in [date]" rather than "Deliver by [date]" — a commitment to report on progress rather than to achieve an outcome. This is honest (progress reports are achievable; infrastructure delivery is slower) but means the timelines cannot be used to assess whether the government's ambitions are being met.

### Gaps

**No funding commitments.** The original plan was criticised for containing no budgets. This response adds timelines but still contains no funding commitments beyond confirming AISI funding at Spending Reviews. The compute expansion, Growth Zones, National Data Library, skills programmes, and sovereign AI function are all endorsed without any indication of cost or budget allocation. The Spending Review is cited as the vehicle for funding decisions, but the document does not indicate what the government intends to request.

**Copyright remains unresolved.** Rec 13 receives the weakest response: "Partially agree" with a commitment to "engage with partner organisations and industry to consider the potential role of government." Given that the IPO working group had already failed to agree a voluntary code (as acknowledged in the Feb 2024 consultation response), this response does not advance the issue.

**No detail on AISI legislation.** The commitment to establish AISI as a statutory body is significant but entirely unspecified. What powers would a statutory AISI have? Would it be a regulator, an evaluator, or both? Would it have enforcement authority? What would its relationship be to sector regulators? The commitment is a single sentence with no supporting detail.

**Workers and labour markets.** Not addressed. The original plan's near-silence on displacement is carried forward unchanged. The Clifford plan's Rec 19 (lifelong skills programme) is agreed, but there is no broader engagement with the labour market impact question. The Future of Work unit that appears in the One Year On report is not mentioned here — it was created later.

**Devolution.** Mentioned once: "DSIT will also work with devolved and local government to identify AI adoption opportunities to drive growth." No detail on what this means in practice or how UK-level and devolved AI strategies will be coordinated.

**International governance.** Not addressed. The document is entirely domestic in orientation. The only international reference is Rec 6 (international compute partnerships) and Rec 41 (learning from Singapore's GovTech). For a document published in the same month as the AI Action Plan, with the Paris AI Action Summit weeks away, the absence of any international governance dimension is striking.

### Feasibility

The document's feasibility is stronger than the original plan's because the tabular format forces specificity. Most responses identify a responsible department (DSIT, DfE, DBT, HMT) and a delivery mechanism (Industrial Strategy, Spending Review, Skills England). This makes it possible to assess whether the institutional capacity exists to deliver.

The most feasible commitments are those that build on existing institutions: AISI expansion (existing team), regulator guidance (existing departments), AI in government (i.AI already operational), and the Industrial Strategy (already in development).

The least feasible is the sovereign AI function, which requires creating a new government capability to partner with frontier AI companies, identify investment opportunities, and support talent relocation — all within a few months. The comparison to Japan's MITI and Singapore's EDB is ambitious given that those institutions took decades to develop.

### Internal Consistency

The document is internally consistent — it follows the plan's structure and responds to each recommendation in order. The "Partially Agree" responses are honest about where the government diverges from the plan (copyright, immigration, private sector targets).

One consistency issue with the broader UK governance landscape: the document commits to establishing AISI as a statutory body while the pro-innovation regulatory framework remains explicitly non-statutory. This creates an asymmetry — the safety institute will have a statutory footing while the regulatory principles it supports do not. How a statutory AISI interacts with non-statutory principles and independent sector regulators is not addressed.

### Coverage

The tabular format ensures complete coverage of all 50 recommendations. The document does not add new commitments beyond those in the plan — it responds to what was proposed rather than expanding scope. This means the plan's gaps (workers, ethics, devolution, environment, international governance) are carried forward unchanged.

The document's added value is the delivery timeline and institutional assignment, not additional policy content.

---

## Overall Assessment

This is a useful accountability document that does what government responses should do: it takes each recommendation, states whether the government agrees, says who will deliver it, and gives a timeline. The tabular format is a model that other UK AI governance documents would benefit from adopting.

Its most significant policy contribution is the commitment to establish AISI as a statutory body and to consult on legislation for regulatory certainty — the first explicit legislative commitment in the UK's AI governance framework. This represents a meaningful evolution from the non-statutory approach proposed in the white paper and maintained through the consultation response.

Its most significant weakness is that it endorses ambition without committing resources. Every recommendation is agreed (or partially agreed), every one has a timeline, but none has a budget. The document creates the appearance of comprehensive delivery planning while deferring the hard decisions — how much will this cost, who pays, and what happens if funding is insufficient — to the Spending Review.

The document is best read alongside the One Year On progress report (January 2026), which provides evidence of what was actually delivered against these commitments. The pairing reveals both genuine delivery (Growth Zones, AISI scaling, skills programmes) and persistent gaps (copyright, international governance, workers, outcome measurement).
