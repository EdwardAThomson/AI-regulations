# FCA AI Update — Review

**Document:** AI Update
**Published:** 22 April 2024 (referenced in the One Year On report as part of sector-specific AI regulation)
**Issuing body:** Financial Conduct Authority (FCA)
**Foreword by:** Jessica Rusu, Chief Data, Information and Intelligence Officer, FCA
**Length:** 25 pages

---

## Summary

This is the FCA's formal response to the UK Government's pro-innovation AI regulation framework, setting out how the existing FCA regulatory toolkit maps onto the five cross-sectoral principles (safety/security/robustness, appropriate transparency/explainability, fairness, accountability/governance, contestability/redress). The FCA positions itself as "a technology-agnostic, principles-based and outcomes-focused regulator" and explicitly opts against AI-specific rules. Instead, it demonstrates how existing frameworks — the Principles for Business, Threshold Conditions, SYSC sourcebook, Senior Managers and Certification Regime (SM&CR), Consumer Duty, operational resilience rules, and Critical Third Parties (CTP) regime — already address AI-related risks. The FCA commits to continued monitoring, a third edition of the joint FCA/Bank of England machine learning survey, the AI & Digital Hub pilot with DRCF, engagement with IOSCO and international forums, and exploration of AI sandbox arrangements. The document is the most substantive UK sector-regulator AI publication and is cited by both the CMA report and the Pro-Innovation Consultation Response.

---

## Key Points

### FCA Position

- **Technology-agnostic, principles-based, outcomes-focused** regulator
- **No AI-specific rules proposed** — instead, mapping existing framework to the government's 5 principles
- Primary statutory objectives (s.1B Financial Services and Markets Act 2000): consumer protection, market integrity, effective competition
- Secondary objective (via Financial Services and Markets Act 2023): international competitiveness and growth (medium to long term)
- Sees "beneficial innovation as a vital component of effective competition"

### Mapping to Government's 5 Principles

**Safety, security, robustness (3.8-3.17):**
- Principles for Business (especially P2 due skill/care/diligence; P3 organise affairs responsibly with adequate risk management)
- Threshold Conditions — business model must be suitable
- SYSC 4 (organisational requirements), SYSC 7 (risk controls), SYSC 15A (Operational Resilience)
- Operational Resilience: firms must keep Important Business Services (IBSs) within Impact Tolerance (ITol) under severe but plausible scenarios
- SYSC 8 / SYSC 13 — outsourcing rules
- **Critical Third Parties regime (CP26/23)** — consultation paper on operational resilience for CTPs. The AI regime "is not specific to AI, but the concept of services a CTP provides is broad enough to encompass considerations around the systemic use of a common AI model (e.g. data bias, model robustness)." "Systemic AI providers" could come within scope if designated by HM Treasury

**Competition concerns (3.18-3.19):**
- Concern about "concentration of third-party technology services, such as cloud services and AI model development, among Big Tech firms"
- Concern that Big Tech could enter partnerships with financial firms on "take-it-or-leave-it basis"
- Engaging with CMA on cloud services market investigation and AI Foundation Models review

**Fairness (3.20-3.33):**
- Consumer Duty (came into force July 2023 for new/existing; July 2024 for closed products)
- Consumer Duty requires firms to "act in good faith, avoid causing foreseeable harm, and enable and support retail customers to pursue their financial objectives"
- Duty addresses "discrimination harms by requiring firms to take account of different customer needs, including those with characteristics of vulnerability and those with protected characteristics"
- AI systems "using AI technologies in a way that embeds or amplifies bias, leading to worse outcomes for some groups of consumers, might not be acting in good faith for their consumers, unless differences in outcome can be justified objectively"
- Vulnerable Customers Guidance (FG22/5)
- UK GDPR and Data Protection Act 2018 — Article 22 on automated decision-making
- Equality Act 2010 (enforced by EHRC, or Equality Commission for Northern Ireland)

**Appropriate transparency and explainability (3.34-3.37):**
- "Whilst our regulatory framework does not specifically address the transparency or explainability of AI systems, there are a number of high-level requirements"
- Consumer Duty Principle 2A.2.2R — act in good faith
- Principle 7 — communicate in way that is clear, fair, not misleading
- UK GDPR Articles 13 and 14 require meaningful information about automated decision-making logic

**Accountability and governance (3.38-3.43):**
- SYSC 4.1.1R — robust governance arrangements, clear organisational structure
- Senior Managers and Certification Regime (SM&CR) — "dedicated Senior Manager responsible for AI within firms" considered but not deemed necessary (existing structures sufficient)
- Technology systems normally under SMF24 (Chief Operations); risk controls under SMF4 (Chief Risk)
- Any use of AI in a function falls within scope of the relevant SMF manager's responsibilities
- Statement of Responsibilities required for SM&CR firms
- Board Champion for Consumer Duty implementation recommended

**Contestability and redress (3.44-3.47):**
- Firms responsible for ensuring compliance regardless of AI involvement
- Complaints handling procedures (DISP sourcebook)
- Financial Ombudsman Service for independent review
- Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS)
- Article 22 UK GDPR right not to be subject to solely automated decisions with legal/significant effects

### 12-Month Plan (Section 4)

- Third edition of joint FCA/Bank of England machine learning survey
- Collaboration with Payment Systems Regulator (PSR) on AI across payment systems
- AI & Digital Hub pilot (with DRCF — CMA, ICO, Ofcom, FCA)
- Digital Sandbox allows testing with synthetic data; Regulatory Sandbox (FCA global pioneer)
- Exploring AI Sandbox — "testing, design, governance and impact of AI technologies"
- International engagement: IOSCO AI working group, Financial Stability Board (FSB), OECD, Global Financial Innovation Network (GFIN), G7
- Research on deepfakes and simulated content via DRCF Horizon Scanning workstream
- Response to Call For Input on data asymmetry between Big Tech and traditional financial services
- Quantum computing monitoring

### FCA's Own Use of AI

- Advanced Analytics unit uses ML for scam websites detection, market surveillance, sanctions screening
- 75+ data scientists recruited; new digital hub in Leeds
- Synthetic Data Expert Group
- ML tools: "33.5% increase in firm warnings 2021-2022"
- TechSprint on trade surveillance using AI

## Observations

### Clarity

The document is clearly structured into four sections (role/objectives, work to date, existing approach, future plans). The paragraph numbering and cross-references make it usable as a reference document. The mapping from government principles to existing FCA rules is systematic and helpful — a reader can trace each principle to specific provisions (SYSC chapters, Consumer Duty, SM&CR elements).

However, the document's length (25 pages with substantial cover art) suggests more substance than it delivers. The core message — "our existing framework already covers AI risks; we will continue to monitor" — could be stated in five pages. The extensive mapping to existing rules, while useful as a reference, does not involve the FCA committing to new action beyond what already exists.

One clarity gap: the document never addresses what the FCA would consider evidence that the existing framework is insufficient. No thresholds, no triggering events, no criteria. The FCA reserves the right to "actively consider future regulatory adaptations if needed" (4.3) but does not specify when that need would be recognised.

### Measurability

Few specific commitments. The measurable elements are:

- Third edition of ML survey (with BoE)
- AI & Digital Hub pilot operational
- AI sandbox exploration
- 75+ data scientists recruited
- 33.5% increase in scam website firm warnings (a backward-looking metric)

The document does not commit to: specific timelines for AI sandbox launch, specific number of firms engaged, specific compliance milestones, any measurable outcome of the AI & Digital Hub, any measurable evaluation of whether the existing framework is working. This is a consistent pattern in UK sector-regulator AI publications — process commitments without outcome measures.

### Gaps

**Enforcement signals absent.** The document describes many mechanisms (Principles, SYSC, Consumer Duty, SM&CR, DISP, Article 22 UK GDPR) but provides no examples of enforcement action against firms using AI inappropriately. The FCA's approach relies on firms interpreting existing rules correctly; there is no case law or enforcement precedent specific to AI to guide that interpretation. An implicit assumption is that firms will behave; the document does not address what happens when they don't.

**Foundation models and generative AI treated lightly.** The document mentions "the rapid rise of Large Language Models" (4.4) once in passing, noting it "makes regulatory regimes, such as those relating to operational resilience, outsourcing and critical third parties even more central." But the specific questions posed by generative AI for financial services — hallucinations, prompt injection, data leakage, AI in customer-facing chatbots, AI-generated financial advice — are not engaged with in detail.

**Critical Third Parties regime conditional.** The document identifies CTPs as the most relevant mechanism for systemic AI providers but notes that designation is HM Treasury's decision. Whether and when major AI providers (OpenAI, Anthropic, Microsoft, Google) would be designated as CTPs is the central question; the document does not address it.

**Big Tech concerns flagged but not addressed.** Paragraph 3.18 identifies concentration risks and "take-it-or-leave-it" partnership dynamics. The FCA's response (3.19) is that it is "engaging with the Competition and Markets Authority." Engagement is not remediation. The question of what the FCA would do if Big Tech providers refused to provide necessary information for oversight is not addressed.

**Consumer redress for AI outcomes.** The document relies on existing complaints infrastructure (DISP, Financial Ombudsman Service, FSCS). These require the complainant to identify that something went wrong. For AI-driven decisions where the logic is opaque, consumers may not know they have grounds for complaint. The document does not propose proactive detection mechanisms for systemic AI-driven consumer harm.

**Workers / labour impact of AI in financial services.** Not addressed. The FCA's remit does not extend to employment protection, but AI's potential to eliminate significant numbers of financial services jobs has consumer protection implications (e.g. access to human advice, call centre automation, branch closures). The document does not engage with this dimension.

**Data protection fragmentation.** The document defers data protection questions to the ICO. But AI in financial services often involves data protection and financial regulation simultaneously. The division of responsibility — ICO for data, FCA for conduct — may create gaps or redundancies. The document does not propose integrated regulatory interventions.

**AI in algorithmic trading.** One paragraph (3.52-3.53) on TechSprint for trade surveillance. But algorithmic trading is a major AI use case with specific risks (market manipulation, flash crashes, herding) that deserve more than a passing reference. The document does not systematically address AI in market infrastructure.

### Feasibility

Highly feasible — the document largely describes what the FCA already does and will continue doing. The specific commitments (ML survey, AI & Digital Hub, international engagement) are modest and within the FCA's existing capability.

The more ambitious implicit commitment — that the existing FCA framework will prove sufficient for AI supervision — is untested. The document asserts it but does not evidence it. The ongoing Call For Input on Big Tech data asymmetry and the continuing CMA work on cloud and foundation models suggest that at least one area (market structure) may require additional FCA action.

### Internal Consistency

Consistent throughout. The message is uniform: existing framework sufficient, continue monitoring, engage internationally, explore sandboxes.

One subtle tension: the FCA emphasises its secondary international competitiveness objective while also noting that international coordination (IOSCO, FSB) may produce standards the UK would need to align with. The document does not address what happens if UK competitiveness and international standards conflict. The UK's distinctive pro-innovation position may be easier to maintain domestically than in international regulatory dialogue.

### Coverage

Coverage within FCA remit is reasonable. The 5-principle mapping is systematic; operational resilience and CTPs get due attention; Consumer Duty is well-developed; SM&CR is explained. FCA's own AI use (section 3.48+) is a useful addition.

Coverage gaps as noted: generative AI specifics, algorithmic trading, workforce impact, proactive consumer detection. The document is better at describing what the FCA does than at engaging with the most challenging questions AI poses for financial services regulation.

### International Alignment

The FCA's international engagement footprint is substantial: IOSCO AI working group, FSB, OECD, GFIN, G7. The document commits to continued engagement and references the AI Safety Summit and G7 Hiroshima AI Process. As a technical sector regulator, the FCA is well-placed to participate in international financial services AI coordination.

The document does not address UK-EU regulatory divergence on AI in financial services. The EU AI Act classifies certain financial services AI uses as high-risk; the UK's context-specific approach does not. Firms operating across UK and EU financial markets face different AI compliance regimes. This cross-border complexity is not engaged with in the document.

---

## Overall Assessment

This is a competent, professional, and thoroughly referenced update from a well-established regulator. It demonstrates that the FCA understands its own toolkit and has mapped it plausibly to the government's 5 principles. The Consumer Duty, SYSC operational resilience, and SM&CR governance provisions are genuinely applicable to AI — the document is not making things up.

Its greatest strength is that it is institutionally grounded. The FCA has real powers, real enforcement history, real supervisory relationships with regulated firms. Unlike AISI (which has no enforcement authority) or the Information Commissioner (which has data protection but not financial conduct powers), the FCA is a regulator with specific obligations it can enforce. Its framing of AI as "one more technology to supervise within existing rules" is credible in a way that it would not be for many other government bodies.

Its greatest weakness is the caution. The document does not commit to any new AI-specific action, does not identify any AI-specific risks that existing rules inadequately address, does not propose any AI-specific enforcement priorities. It reads as a document written to demonstrate the FCA is taking AI seriously rather than to actually change FCA practice.

The 12-month plan (section 4) underlines this. The substantive commitments — continued monitoring, survey, hub pilot, international engagement — are a minimal programme of work that could have been undertaken without publication. The more ambitious element — exploring AI sandbox arrangements — is "exploring" rather than launching. (The FCA's Supercharged Sandbox did launch subsequently in October 2024, per the AI Opportunities Action Plan One Year On, which is a concrete deliverable against this commitment.)

The document's positioning is consistent with the broader UK approach: existing frameworks apply, regulators retain discretion, no AI-specific legislation. Whether this approach proves sufficient as frontier AI is increasingly deployed in financial services — for trading, underwriting, advice, customer interaction, fraud detection, compliance monitoring — will be tested over the coming years. The FCA has reserved the right to adapt, but has not committed to specific triggers for adaptation.

The most useful element of the document for external readers is the systematic mapping from principles to existing rules (paragraphs 3.7-3.47). A firm deploying AI in UK financial services can use this document as a checklist for existing regulatory requirements, which is a genuine contribution to compliance clarity. As a policy document, however, the Update reads more as a defence of the status quo than as a programme for adaptation.
