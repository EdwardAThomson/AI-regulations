# Delivering AI Growth Zones — Review

**Document:** Delivering AI Growth Zones
**Published:** November 2025 (CP 1440)
**Issuing body:** Department for Science, Innovation and Technology (DSIT)
**Presented to Parliament by:** The Secretary of State for Science, Innovation and Technology by Command of His Majesty
**Length:** 15 pages

---

## Summary

This is the UK Government's detailed policy paper on delivering the AI Growth Zone (AIGZ) concept announced in the January 2025 AI Opportunities Action Plan. Where the Action Plan proposed AIGZs as "areas with enhanced access to power and support for planning approvals" for AI data centre development, this document specifies the implementation package across six chapters: introduction, grid connections, energy prices, planning, people/places, and investor environment. The headline claims are substantial: reduce time to power by up to five years, save a 500 MW data centre up to 80 million annually on electricity, unlock up to 100 billion of additional investment, and create over ten thousand jobs. The operational mechanisms include new grid connection prioritisation, pricing support delivering up to 24 GBP/MWh discounts in Scotland, planning system reform (National Planning Policy Framework update, National Policy Statement for Data Centres, 4.5 million for planning experts), 100% business rate retention for 25 years, 5 million per zone for local AI adoption, and a cross-government AI Growth Zone Delivery Unit launching January 2026.

---

## Key Points

### Headline Commitments

- **Up to 5 years** reduced time to power
- **Up to 80 million GBP annual savings** for a 500 MW data centre
- **Up to 100 billion GBP additional investment** unlocked
- **More than 10,000 jobs** created
- **AI Growth Zone Delivery Unit** launching January 2026

### Grid Connections (Chapter 2)

- **New reallocation mechanism** — gives priority access to freed-up capacity when another project exits the grid connection queue
- **New reservation mechanism** — allows capacity to be held at specific physical connection points for AIGZs or other strategically important projects
- **Planning and Infrastructure Bill** to prioritise projects identified by government as strategically important
- **Strategic plan to remove speculative demand projects** from the connections queue
- **Working with Ofgem and NESO** (National Energy System Operator) to update technical codes and licences
- **Enabling developer self-build** — mechanisms for developers to construct high-voltage lines and substations themselves. Models include "build and transfer" (hand over to network operator) and "build and operate" (developer retains ownership)
- **Connections Accelerator Service** — pilot service live before end of 2025; all AIGZ data centre projects qualify

### Energy Prices (Chapter 3)

**Pricing support mechanism** recycling grid constraint cost savings to projects delivering measurable system benefits:
- Up to **24 GBP/MWh discount in Scotland**
- Up to **16 GBP/MWh in Cumbria**
- Up to **14 GBP/MWh in North East**
- From April 2027, subject to legislative timetables
- Review point in 2030
- Exemption from portion of electricity system costs
- "No additional cost for other electricity billpayers"
- Rebalances investment to regions with available clean energy — addresses constraint cost problem (Scottish wind generation exceeding grid transmission capacity)

### Planning Reform (Chapter 4)

- **Update National Planning Policy Framework** — consultation on changes in next three months
- **National Policy Statement for Data Centres** — providing clarity on needs case, parameters, thresholds. Will consider whether Critical National Priority status should apply to Data Centres (as with low carbon energy)
- **National team of AI data centre planning experts** backed by 4.5 million — direct advice and grant funding to Local Planning Authorities
- **Safeguarding powers** to protect AIGZ land from conflicting development
- **Consultation Direction update** — awareness of relevant AI infrastructure planning applications; Secretary of State "call-in" powers
- **Nationally Significant Infrastructure Projects (NSIP) route** — exploring whether to cut consent timelines from 18 to 12 months
- **"Floor not ceiling"** — further reforms under consideration, including wider reform of call-in powers

### Opportunities for People and Places (Chapter 5)

- **187 million GBP TechFirst programme** — build AI-native workforce
- Short courses in AI through the Growth and Skills Levy
- Exploring new qualification in data science and AI for 16-19 year olds (alongside existing T Level)
- **Five Digital and Technologies Technical Excellence Colleges (TECs)**
- **Up to 5 million GBP per AI Growth Zone** for local AI adoption, workforce building, research and development
- **100% business rate retention for 25 years** for local authorities in England with AIGZs (via MHCLG secondary legislation, effective April 2027)
- Estimated 5-10 million GBP per site additional annual local authority revenue once built

### Investment Environment (Chapter 6)

- **AI Growth Zone Delivery Unit** within DSIT — formal launch January 2026
- Two teams:
  - **Pipeline team** — identifying sites and investors; works with Office for Investment
  - **Delivery team** — dedicated Account Manager and Taskforce per zone; modelled on North East Combined Authority approach; two workstreams (local benefits; developers/utilities/government coordination)
- **National Wealth Fund and UK Export Finance** exploring co-financing for AIGZ projects

## Observations

### Clarity

The document is short (15 pages), well-structured, and clearly written. The six-chapter architecture maps cleanly to the interventions described. Each chapter opens with an Overview followed by "Action we are taking" broken into sub-sections. The writing is direct and operational rather than rhetorical — a reader can extract commitments without wading through justifications.

The executive structure of the introduction (introducing five chapters that follow) allows the reader to preview the intervention package before engaging with details. The "floor not ceiling" framing (end of Chapter 4) signals that further reform is under consideration, inviting industry input.

The document's biggest clarity virtue is that it treats AIGZs as a specific delivery problem — data centres, grid, planning, pricing — rather than as a general industrial strategy theme. This focus makes the policy proposals specific and assessable.

### Measurability

Strong on input measures, modest on outcome measures:

**Measurable inputs:**
- 4.5 million GBP for planning experts team
- 5 million GBP per AIGZ for local AI adoption
- 187 million GBP TechFirst programme
- 100% business rate retention for 25 years
- 24/16/14 GBP/MWh pricing discounts
- Planning reform timelines (3 months for consultation; 12-18 months for NSIP)
- 2027 commencement for pricing mechanism; 2030 review
- AIGZ Delivery Unit launching January 2026

**Claimed outcome measures:**
- 5-year time to power reduction
- 80M GBP annual savings (500 MW data centre)
- 100 billion GBP additional investment
- 10,000+ jobs
- 5-10M GBP per site annual local authority revenue

The outcome figures are presented without methodology. How was "up to 100 billion" calculated? What's the counterfactual? Which zones contribute what share? The figures serve political communication rather than evaluation — they are large headline numbers without specified evidence base.

The document does not propose monitoring or evaluation of AIGZ programme outcomes. No success metrics for zones, no performance indicators for the Delivery Unit, no public reporting cadence. The 2030 review of the pricing mechanism is the only scheduled evaluation moment mentioned.

### Gaps

**Workers and communities beyond jobs.** The "opportunities for people and places" chapter emphasises skills and business rate retention. But data centre construction and operation employ few people relative to investment scale (large data centres typically employ hundreds, not thousands, of full-time staff). The 10,000+ jobs figure likely includes construction and indirect employment; the document does not break this down. Communities hosting AIGZs may face noise, traffic, infrastructure burden, water use, and visual impact without corresponding local employment. The 5M per zone for local adoption is modest relative to likely local burden.

**Environmental impact quantification absent.** This is a document about building large data centres. Data centre water use, local emissions, grid carbon intensity, and embedded carbon in construction are all material. The document mentions "strengthen the grid and lower system costs" via locations near available clean energy (Scottish wind), which is sensible, but provides no environmental targets or reporting obligations. The AI Opportunities Action Plan One Year On references Small Modular Reactors and micro-grids near AIGZs but this document does not engage substantively with environmental regulation.

**Devolution treatment uneven.** Scotland and Cumbria and the North East get specific pricing support (24/16/14 GBP/MWh respectively). Wales gets an AIGZ (one of the five designated in the One Year On report). But the document is silent on coordination with devolved governments on planning, skills, or local benefits in Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland. Business rate retention is explicitly "in England" — the devolution equivalent is not addressed. Planning reform is English Planning Act based (Town and Country Planning Act, NSIP regime via Planning and Infrastructure Bill); Scottish/Welsh planning systems are not engaged with.

**Which tenants, which technologies.** The document is technology-neutral within AI: it does not distinguish between training-heavy and inference-heavy workloads, between frontier model development and enterprise AI inference, between UK-based tenants and foreign tenants. AIGZs are presented as infrastructure for "AI" writ large. Whether a zone hosting a US hyperscaler training frontier models for a US parent company delivers the same "sovereign compute" benefit as a UK-tenanted facility is not addressed.

**National security dimension absent.** Data centres hosting foundation model training contain substantial strategic value. The document does not address national security screening of AIGZ tenants, data sovereignty requirements, or export control implications. The Sovereign AI Unit (referenced in the AI Opportunities Action Plan) is presumably the relevant mechanism; this document does not link to it.

**Pricing support sustainability.** The 24/16/14 GBP/MWh discounts come from "recycling grid constraint cost savings." These savings exist while Scottish renewable generation exceeds grid transmission capacity. If transmission is upgraded (as grid policy will eventually require), the savings disappear and the pricing support becomes a subsidy. The 2030 review point acknowledges this but the underlying dynamic is not engaged with.

**Competition concerns.** The CMA's 2023 foundation models report flagged concerns about CSP concentration (AWS, Azure, GCP) and partnership arrangements. AIGZ tenancy is likely to be dominated by CSPs or CSP-adjacent developers. The document does not address how AIGZ policy interacts with CMA competition scrutiny — whether, for example, CSPs would be prohibited from exclusive arrangements in AIGZs, or whether competition conditions would apply to zone allocation.

**Long-term land use.** 25-year business rate retention signals a long time horizon. What happens after? Data centres are long-lived assets but AI workload characteristics could change rapidly. The document does not address repurposing, decommissioning, or land return.

### Feasibility

Most interventions are feasible within existing government capability:

- **Grid reform** — requires Ofgem and NESO cooperation, which the document describes as already underway
- **Pricing support** — legislative subject to Parliamentary time; mechanism described is technically achievable
- **Planning reform** — consultation within 3 months; NPPF update is established process
- **National Planning team** — 4.5 million is modest funding, feasible
- **Delivery Unit** — within DSIT, launching January 2026 — already essentially established per the One Year On report
- **Business rate retention** — MHCLG secondary legislation, effective April 2027

The more ambitious claims are less clearly feasible:
- **5-year time to power reduction** — depends on grid, planning, and construction moving in sync. Grid connection queues are a substantial engineering/commercial problem that cannot be solved by policy alone
- **100 billion investment** — depends on private investor decisions over time horizons
- **10,000+ jobs** — depends on AIGZ scale-up actually happening; AIGZ designations under the Action Plan's 28.2 billion claim (One Year On) already claim 15,000 jobs, so this figure may be additive or may overlap

The feasibility of the broader programme depends on AIGZs actually attracting investment. The January 2026 One Year On report claims five zones designated with 28.2B GBP investment; whether this translates into built capacity on the timescales claimed remains to be seen.

### Internal Consistency

Internally consistent. The six chapters address the identified barriers (grid, energy prices, planning, people, investors) systematically. The interventions reinforce each other — pricing support in renewable-rich regions, planning reform to accelerate construction, grid reform to connect sites, Delivery Unit to coordinate across government.

One consistency issue with broader UK AI governance: the AI for Science Strategy (November 2025, published around the same time as this document) emphasises sovereign compute for UK research priorities. The AI Opportunities Action Plan emphasises sovereign AI capability. This document is essentially data centre industrial policy — attracting investment from whoever will invest. The relationship between "sovereign compute for UK research," "sovereign AI for UK strategic interests," and "data centre investment policy regardless of tenant" is not addressed in any of these documents. The three framings coexist; whether they are compatible is unclear.

### Coverage

Coverage within scope is reasonable for a 15-page policy paper. Grid, pricing, planning, skills/local, investment environment — the obvious intervention categories for data centre investment. The document does not pretend to cover AI regulation, safety, ethics, or workforce impact beyond skills.

Coverage gaps (discussed above): environmental, devolution depth, national security, competition, long-term land use.

### International Alignment

The document is almost entirely domestic. International comparators would be useful — Ireland's data centre strategy (and the subsequent moratorium on new data centres in Dublin), Singapore's green data centre standards, US state-level data centre subsidies (Virginia, Texas) — but are not engaged with. The UK AIGZ proposition is presented on its own terms.

The pricing support mechanism (reducing electricity costs below wholesale for eligible data centres) is a significant market intervention that could attract state aid scrutiny under WTO rules or under UK's own Subsidy Control Act 2022. The document does not address this dimension.

---

## Overall Assessment

This is a focused, operational policy paper that does what strategy documents often fail to do: specify concrete interventions with named mechanisms, costs, timelines, and delivery structures. The AI Growth Zone concept has been translated from the January 2025 Action Plan rhetoric into a deliverable programme of work. The 4.5 million for planning experts, the specific pricing discounts (24/16/14 GBP/MWh), the three-month consultation on NPPF, the January 2026 Delivery Unit launch — these are concrete commitments with short time horizons.

Its greatest strength is specificity. Compared to the AI Opportunities Action Plan (which proposed AIGZs without defining them), the One Year On (which counted AIGZs as a success without specifying deliverables), and sector documents (which refer to AIGZs in passing), this document actually says what the government will do to make AIGZs happen. The intervention package spans genuine policy levers — Ofgem, NESO, planning system, electricity pricing, business rates, central coordination.

Its greatest weakness is the treatment of externalities. The document assumes that data centre investment in chosen locations is good; it provides little consideration of communities affected, environmental impact, competition effects, national security screening, or long-term land use. The 100 billion GBP investment claim is impressive; what happens if 100 billion of data centre capacity is built is left as an implicit "good thing."

The regional pricing support is a significant policy innovation that deserves attention. It uses grid constraint cost savings to direct data centre investment to renewable-rich regions — a creative re-use of an existing inefficiency. Whether this mechanism survives Subsidy Control Act scrutiny and whether it produces the claimed rebalancing outcomes over the 2027-2030 window will test the approach.

The AIGZ Delivery Unit is potentially important as a coordinating structure, but like the Future of Work unit discussed in the AI Opportunities Action Plan One Year On, its establishment is easier to report than its outcomes. The North East Combined Authority model it references is a working precedent; whether that model scales to a cross-government unit coordinating investors, developers, utilities, planning authorities, and local communities remains to be tested.

For a country that has named AI as an economic priority and committed 2+ billion in aggregate funding, this document is the most operational expression of what the infrastructure delivery side of that commitment looks like. The reviews of the AI Opportunities Action Plan, One Year On report, and Government Response all noted the gap between ambition and delivery; this document narrows that gap on one specific dimension (data centre infrastructure). It does not engage with the broader questions that AI governance raises, and nor should it — it is an infrastructure policy paper, and it executes that brief well.
