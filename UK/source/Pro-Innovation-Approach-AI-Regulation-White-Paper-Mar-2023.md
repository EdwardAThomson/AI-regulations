## Ministerial Foreword

The Secretary of State positions AI as a transformative technology and the UK as "the best place in the world to build, test and use AI technology." Key framing:

- The UK has invested over 2.5 billion pounds in AI since 2014
- Recent commitments include 110 million for the AI Tech Missions Fund, 900 million for AI research infrastructure, 8 million for the Global Talent Network, and 117 million for new AI PhDs
- AI's transformative potential spans healthcare, transport, and scientific discovery, alongside risks including physical harm, national security threats, and mental health impacts
- Post-Brexit regulatory sovereignty is explicitly cited: "having exited the European Union we are free to establish a regulatory approach" distinct from other jurisdictions
- Commits to regulatory sandboxes to help AI products reach market

---

## Executive Summary

### The Opportunity and the Challenge

AI delivers "wide societal benefits, from medical advances to mitigating climate change." The UK ranks third globally in AI investment, innovation, and implementation, hosting "a third of Europe's total AI companies." The Science and Technology Framework identifies AI as one of five critical technologies.

Cross-cutting risks requiring intervention include: physical and mental health damage, privacy infringement and human rights violations, bias and discrimination, national security threats from automated cyber attacks, and societal harm including threats to democratic integrity. "Public trust in AI will be undermined unless these risks...are addressed."

### The Framework

Five cross-sectoral principles:

1. **Safety, security and robustness**
2. **Appropriate transparency and explainability**
3. **Fairness**
4. **Accountability and governance**
5. **Contestability and redress**

The principles will initially be "issued on a non-statutory basis" to avoid "rigid and onerous legislative requirements." The government anticipates "introducing a statutory duty on regulators requiring them to have due regard to the principles" following an implementation review.

Central support functions will include: monitoring framework effectiveness, assessing cross-sector AI risks, horizon scanning, testbed and sandbox support, business and citizen education, and international regulatory alignment. The framework "focuses on the use of AI rather than the technology itself."

## Part 1: Introduction

### 1.1 The Power and Potential of Artificial Intelligence

The UK AI sector employs over 50,000 people and contributes 3.7 billion pounds to the economy. UK universities produce leading AI research and talent. The UK is home to the third largest number of AI unicorns and start-ups in the world.

Current applications include traffic management, fraud detection, nuclear fusion control, medicine discovery, and climate research. Recent large language models demonstrate capabilities across code generation, genetic medicine, and document analysis. AI is identified as potentially impactful as "electricity or the internet."

**Examples of AI opportunities cited:**
- AlphaFold solving protein-folding, advancing disease research
- Deep learning in breast cancer screening accelerating diagnosis
- Autonomous robots improving agricultural field mapping and soil management
- AI accelerating pharmaceutical discovery from millions of candidate compounds
- Child exploitation databases using AI for victim identification
- Pattern-recognition for cybersecurity threat response

### 1.2 Managing AI Risks

"Not all AI risks arise from the deliberate action of bad actors" — many emerge from "lack of appropriate controls."

**Risk categories identified:**
- **Safety:** physical damage to humans and property, damage to mental health
- **Security:** risks to individuals, organisations, and critical infrastructure
- **Fairness:** unfairness in the justice system, discriminatory lending
- **Privacy:** surveillance creating a "near-complete portrait of an individual's home life"
- **Societal wellbeing:** disinformation undermining "trust in democratic institutions"

**Illustrative risk scenarios:** deepfake pornography, LLM assistants recommending dangerous activities, credit tools discriminating by race or gender, home surveillance building behavioural profiles, disinformation campaigns, automated cyber attacks via phishing.

### 1.3 Terminology

Key definitions established:
- **AI/AI system:** Products and services that are "adaptable" and "autonomous"
- **AI supplier:** Organisations involved in research, development, training, deployment, or sale of AI
- **AI user:** Individuals or organisations using AI products
- **AI life cycle:** All events from inception to decommissioning
- **Foundation model:** AI trained on vast data, adaptable for multiple tasks
- **Impacted third party:** Individuals affected by AI outcomes they do not use or supply

## Part 2: The Current Regulatory Environment

### 2.1 Navigating the Current Landscape

UK laws already address some AI risks through: the Equality Act 2010 (discriminatory outcomes), data protection (fair processing), product safety legislation (manufactured goods), product-specific rules (electrical equipment, medical devices, toys), consumer rights law (sales contracts), and tort law (civil redress).

**Identified gaps:** "Some AI risks arise across, or in the gaps between, existing regulatory remits." Industry feedback indicates the "absence of cross-cutting AI regulation creates uncertainty," regulatory gaps "undermine business and consumer confidence," and regulatory incoherence could "stifle innovation and competition." Smaller businesses (majority employing under 50 people) are disproportionately burdened.

**Case study: AI fairness in insurance.** A fictional insurance company designing AI pricing algorithms faces regulatory uncertainty — fairness could be addressed through data protection, equality law, consumer protection, and financial services rules, but "it can be challenging...to identify which rules are relevant."

**Case study: MHRA adaptation.** The Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency exemplifies proactive adaptation, publishing 2022 guidance on AI and software in medical devices, developing guidance on transparency and interpretability, and coordinating with ICO and National Data Guardian.

**Coordination challenge:** While some regulators collaborate through the Digital Regulation Cooperation Forum (DRCF), others have "limited capacity and access to AI expertise," risking "inconsistent enforcement."

## Part 3: An Innovative and Iterative Approach

### 3.1 Aims of the Regulatory Framework

"Product safety legislation has increased innovation towards safer products and services." AI regulation can similarly strengthen "public trust and increase AI adoption."

**Three core objectives:**

1. **Drive growth and prosperity** — reduce regulatory uncertainty, support responsible innovation, act "quickly to remove existing barriers," give "UK innovators a headstart in the global race"
2. **Increase public trust in AI** — address risks and protect fundamental values. Trust is "a critical driver for AI adoption"
3. **Strengthen global leadership** — shape international governance, support "the development of the global AI assurance industry," "promote interoperability with other regulatory approaches and minimise cross-border frictions"

**Scope limitations:** The framework does not address access to data, compute capability, sustainability, or content creator rights vs. developer interests — acknowledged as important but outside regulatory scope. Government is separately considering IP implications for generative AI.

### 3.2 The Proposed Regulatory Framework

**Defining characteristics:** pro-innovation, proportionate, trustworthy, adaptable, clear, collaborative.

**Four key elements:**

1. Defining AI based on unique characteristics
2. Adopting context-specific approaches
3. Providing cross-sectoral principles
4. Delivering central support functions

#### 3.2.1 Defining Artificial Intelligence

"There is no general definition of AI that enjoys widespread consensus." Rather than rigid legal definitions that become "quickly outdated," the framework uses two functional characteristics:

- **Adaptivity:** AI systems are "trained" on data patterns "often not easily discernible to humans." Through training, systems "develop the ability to perform new forms of inference not directly envisioned by their human programmers."
- **Autonomy:** "Some AI systems can make decisions without the express intent or ongoing control of a human."

"The combination of adaptivity and autonomy can make it difficult to explain, predict, or control the outputs of an AI system." This is "particularly serious...when decisions are made relating to significant matters, like an individual's health." The functional approach "future-proofs" the framework against "unanticipated new technologies."

#### 3.2.2 Regulating the Use — Not the Technology

The framework is context-specific: "We will not assign rules or risk levels to entire sectors or technologies. Instead, we will regulate based on the outcomes AI is likely to generate in particular applications." An AI chatbot for customer service at a clothing retailer should not be regulated the same as one used in medical diagnostics.

A context-specific approach allows regulators to "weigh the risks of using AI against the costs of missing opportunities to do so." Regulators noted that AI risk assessments should include the failure to exploit AI capabilities — there can be significant opportunity costs from not using AI in safety-critical operations.

Existing UK regulators are empowered to apply the cross-cutting principles. Regulators are best placed to conduct risk assessments within their domains. They will interpret and apply the principles proportionately within their existing remits.

**Foundation models** present particular challenges. "A chatbot that summarises news articles may present very different risks to a chatbot that provides medical advice, even if both are built on the same foundation model." The framework will "monitor developments in foundation models closely" because the same model can underpin applications with very different risk profiles. The framework does not propose to regulate foundation models directly at this stage, but acknowledges that "the pace of technological development could require a change in approach."

#### 3.2.3 Cross-Sectoral Principles

The five principles are intended to provide coherence across regulatory domains while allowing sector-specific implementation:

**1. Safety, security and robustness**
AI systems should function in a robust, secure, and safe way throughout their life cycle, and risks should be continually identified, assessed, and managed. This includes: ensuring AI does not pose unreasonable safety risks; functioning reliably as intended; having appropriate mechanisms to assess and manage risks; being resilient to attempts to alter use or performance by unauthorised third parties.

**2. Appropriate transparency and explainability**
AI systems should be appropriately transparent and explainable. Appropriate transparency means communicating to affected parties about when AI is used, how, and the nature of the decision being supported. Explainability means making the functioning of an AI system intelligible to those who need to understand it. The degree of transparency and explainability should be proportionate to the level of risk.

**3. Fairness**
AI systems should not undermine the legal rights of individuals or organisations, discriminate unfairly, or create unfair market outcomes. This includes: ensuring AI does not generate unfairly discriminatory outputs; that design and use respect the right to privacy; adequate consideration of the impact on third parties; and that AI does not distort competitive markets.

**4. Accountability and governance**
Governance measures should be in place to ensure effective oversight of AI supply and use. There should be appropriate allocation of responsibility for outcomes. Those deploying AI should ensure adequate governance structures, appropriate human oversight, and clear accountability for AI-related decisions.

**5. Contestability and redress**
Where appropriate, affected parties should be able to contest an AI decision or outcome that is harmful or creates material risk of harm. There should be clear routes for redress. The extent of contestability should be proportionate to the severity of harm.

Regulators will implement these principles within their existing remits. The government expects regulators to produce tailored guidance on how the principles apply in their sectors.

#### 3.2.4 Central Support Functions

Individual regulators cannot address all AI risks alone — some risks cut across sectors. The government will establish central functions to:

1. **Monitor and evaluate** the overall regulatory framework's effectiveness
2. **Assess and monitor risks** across the whole economy, identifying cross-cutting and systemic risks
3. **Conduct horizon scanning** to anticipate future developments that may require regulatory adaptation
4. **Support testbeds and sandboxes** to help innovators navigate regulatory requirements, including multi-regulator sandboxes for cross-cutting AI applications
5. **Provide education and awareness** to help businesses understand rules and help citizens understand AI use
6. **Promote interoperability** with international regulatory approaches

These functions will be delivered within existing government structures rather than through a new dedicated AI regulator. The government will consider whether a central government team or a more independent body is the most appropriate long-term mechanism.

#### 3.2.5 Foundation Models

Foundation models present specific regulatory challenges because: they can be adapted for a wide range of tasks with varying risk profiles; a single flaw in a foundation model could propagate across multiple applications; the developer may not anticipate all downstream uses; and the cost and expertise required to develop them may concentrate market power.

The government does not propose to regulate foundation models directly at this stage. Instead: sector regulators will address risks at the application level; the central functions will monitor foundation model developments; and the government will keep the approach under review as the technology evolves.

However, the document signals awareness that this position may need to change: "As foundation models become more powerful and prevalent, we may need to consider targeted measures."

### 3.3 Sandbox and Testbed Support

The government commits to establishing regulatory sandboxes for AI. Four sandbox models are described:

1. **Single sector, single regulator** — simplest to implement
2. **Single sector, multiple regulator** — for AI applications governed by multiple regulators within one sector
3. **Multiple sector, single regulator** — for regulators whose remit crosses sectors
4. **Multiple sector, multiple regulator** — most complex but addresses cross-cutting AI applications

The consultation asks which models would best support innovation and which sectors would benefit most.

## Part 4: Tools for Trustworthy AI

### 4.1 AI Assurance Techniques

"Tools for trustworthy AI including assurance techniques and technical standards will play a critical role in enabling the responsible adoption of AI." The government has published a Roadmap to an effective AI assurance ecosystem and established the UK AI Standards Hub.

Assurance techniques include impact assessment, audit, performance testing, and formal verification. The emerging market for AI assurance services is identified as a UK growth opportunity. The government will launch a Portfolio of AI Assurance Techniques in spring 2023, collaborating with industry to showcase real-world applications.

### 4.2 AI Technical Standards

Multiple international standards development organisations are developing AI-specific technical standards addressing risk management, transparency, bias, safety, and robustness. The government proposes a "layered approach" to applying technical standards:

- **Layer 1:** Sector-agnostic standards (management systems, risk management, quality) providing common foundations across regulatory remits
- **Layer 2:** Issue-specific standards (bias, transparency) adapted to specific risks, complemented by sector-specific guidance
- **Layer 3:** Sector-specific technical standards supporting compliance with specific regulatory requirements

The UK plays a leading role in international standards development, including through ISO/IEC and the British Standards Institution (BSI).

---

## Part 5: Territorial Application

The AI regulation framework applies to the whole of the UK. AI impacts policy areas that are both reserved and devolved. The non-statutory framework will not alter current territorial arrangements, relying on existing legislation on reserved matters (Data Protection Act 2018, Equality Act 2010) for implementation.

The government will engage devolved administrations on the central functions and on potential implications of introducing a statutory duty on regulators. The framework will not change the territorial applicability of existing AI-relevant legislation.

## Part 6: Global Interoperability and International Engagement

The UK is "a global leader in AI" ranked third in the world for AI publications and third for number of AI companies. The framework aims to make the UK "the best place to research AI and to create and build innovative AI companies."

**Key commitments:**
- Promote interoperability and coherence between different national approaches
- Challenge barriers to businesses operating internationally
- Pursue inclusive, multi-stakeholder approaches
- Support other nations to implement regulation and standards
- Use Free Trade Agreements to address challenges AI innovators face

**Multilateral engagement includes:**
- **OECD AI Governance Working Party (AI-GO):** Active member supporting OECD AI Principles implementation
- **Global Partnership on AI (GPAI):** Founding member of the 29-country multilateral forum. UK announced 1.2 million for a Net Zero Data Space for AI Applications plus 1 million for data justice research via the Alan Turing Institute
- **G7:** Working with Japan's 2023 Presidency on international collaboration
- **Council of Europe Committee on AI (CAI):** UK holds a Bureau position, working on the proposed Convention on AI
- **UNESCO:** Involved in development of Ethics of AI Recommendations
- **Global standards organisations:** Leading role in ISO/IEC on 4 active AI projects; member of OCEANIS through BSI

**Bilateral engagement** with the EU, US, Canada, Singapore, Japan, Australia, Israel, Norway, Switzerland, and others.

## Part 7: Conclusion and Next Steps

### First 6 months after publication:
- Engage through the consultation period
- Publish government response to consultation
- Issue cross-sectoral principles to regulators with initial guidance
- Design and publish an AI Regulation Roadmap including plans for central functions and a pilot sandbox
- Analyse findings from commissioned research on compliance barriers, life cycle accountability, regulator capability, and risk measurement

### 6 to 12 months after publication:
- Agree partnership arrangements for delivering first central functions
- Encourage key regulators to publish guidance on applying principles within their remit
- Publish proposals for central monitoring and evaluation framework for consultation
- Continue developing regulatory sandbox with innovators and regulators

### 12 months or more after publication:
- Deliver first iteration of all central functions
- Work with remaining regulators to publish principle-application guidance
- Publish draft central cross-economy AI risk register for consultation
- Develop regulatory sandbox drawing on pilot insights
- Publish first monitoring and evaluation report, including considerations on the need for statutory interventions
- Publish updated AI Regulation Roadmap assessing whether a central government team or more independent body should oversee the central functions long-term
