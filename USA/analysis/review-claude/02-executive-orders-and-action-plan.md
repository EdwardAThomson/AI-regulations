# US Executive Orders and AI Action Plan — Review

A combined review of three documents that together define US federal AI policy as of mid-2025:

1. **EO 14110** — Safe, Secure, and Trustworthy Development and Use of AI (Biden, 30 October 2023) — **REVOKED**
2. **EO 14179** — Removing Barriers to American Leadership in AI (Trump, 23 January 2025) — **CURRENT**
3. **America's AI Action Plan** (White House, July 2025) — **CURRENT**

These documents are reviewed together because they form a before/after narrative: the Biden EO set a safety-first direction; the Trump EO revoked it and set a competitiveness-first direction; the Action Plan operationalises the Trump vision.

---

## EO 14110 — Biden (October 2023, REVOKED)

**Status:** Revoked on 23 January 2025, day one of the Trump administration.

### What it did

EO 14110 was the most comprehensive US executive action on AI. It established eight guiding principles:

1. AI must be safe and secure — requiring robust evaluations, testing, and content labelling
2. Responsible innovation, competition, and collaboration — investment in AI education, protection of small developers
3. Support for American workers — collective bargaining, no undermining of rights, no undue surveillance
4. Equity and civil rights — AI must not deepen discrimination; referenced Blueprint for AI Bill of Rights
5. Consumer protection — enforcement of existing laws in AI context
6. Privacy and civil liberties — limiting extraction and re-identification of personal data
7. Responsible government use — leading by example in federal AI deployment
8. International leadership — promoting responsible AI globally

### Key mandatory requirements (now revoked)

- **Dual-use foundation model reporting**: companies developing models above specified compute thresholds had to notify the government and share safety test results
- **Red-teaming requirements**: mandatory pre-deployment red-teaming for certain models
- **Watermarking**: directed development of content authentication and watermarking standards
- **Agency actions**: directed multiple federal agencies to develop AI-specific rules (HHS for healthcare, DOT for transport, DOE for critical infrastructure, etc.)
- **Immigration**: streamlined visa pathways for AI talent
- **OMB Memoranda**: directed OMB to issue guidance on federal AI procurement and governance (resulting in M-24-10 and M-24-18)

### Historical significance

EO 14110 was the closest the US came to a comprehensive federal AI governance framework. It was widely cited internationally, influenced the EU AI Act's GPAI provisions (particularly compute-based reporting thresholds), and established the US AISI at NIST. Its revocation is itself significant — demonstrating the fragility of executive-order-based AI governance.

## EO 14179 — Trump (January 2025, CURRENT)

**Status:** Current. Signed 23 January 2025.

### What it does

A short executive order (6 sections) that:

1. **Affirms US AI dominance** as a policy goal: "sustain and enhance America's global AI dominance in order to promote human flourishing, economic competitiveness, and national security"
2. **Revokes EO 14110** and all policies issued pursuant to it
3. **Directs AI Action Plan development** within 180 days (resulted in July 2025 Action Plan)
4. **Directs OMB** to revise Biden-era OMB memoranda M-24-10 and M-24-18 within 60 days
5. **Adopts statutory AI definition** from 15 U.S.C. 9401(3)
6. Orders agencies to review and suspend/revise/rescind any actions from EO 14110 inconsistent with new policy

### Framing

The order frames AI governance as a **competitiveness and national security** issue rather than a safety and rights issue. It states AI systems should be "free from ideological bias or engineered social agendas." This philosophical reorientation is the most consequential aspect — it shifts the US posture from "manage risks first" to "remove barriers to innovation first."

## America's AI Action Plan (July 2025, CURRENT)

**Status:** Current. Published July 2025, signed by Michael Kratsios (OSTP), David Sacks (AI and Crypto Advisor), Marco Rubio (NSA).

### Three Pillars

#### Pillar I — Accelerate AI Innovation

**Remove Red Tape and Onerous Regulation**
- Rescind Biden-era regulations
- OSTP to launch RFI on federal regulations hindering AI innovation
- OMB to work with agencies to identify and repeal AI-hindering regulations
- **Funding conditionality**: OMB to work with agencies to "consider a state's AI regulatory climate when making funding decisions and limit funding if the state's AI regulatory regimes may hinder the effectiveness of that funding" — this is a significant federal lever against state-level AI regulation (e.g., Colorado AI Act)
- Review FTC investigations from prior administration to ensure they "do not advance theories of liability that unduly burden AI innovation"

**Ensure Frontier AI Protects Free Speech and American Values**
- **Direct NIST to revise the AI RMF** to "eliminate references to misinformation, Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion, and climate change"
- Update federal procurement guidelines to ensure government contracts only with LLM developers whose systems are "objective and free from top-down ideological bias"
- NIST to evaluate Chinese frontier models for "alignment with Chinese Communist Party talking points and censorship"

**Encourage Open-Source and Open-Weight AI**
- Improve financial markets for compute (spot and forward markets)
- Partner with tech companies for researcher access via NAIRR pilot
- NTIA to convene stakeholders for SME adoption of open models

**Enable AI Adoption**
- "Try-first" culture for AI across American industry
- Address distrust, regulatory complexity, and lack of governance standards

**Empower American Workers**
- AI will "complement" not "replace" workers
- No specific worker protection measures detailed

**Other innovation actions:**
- Next-generation manufacturing
- AI-enabled science (NSF, DOE)
- World-class scientific datasets
- AI interpretability, control, and robustness research
- AI evaluations ecosystem
- Accelerate government AI adoption
- DOD AI adoption
- Protect AI IP
- Combat synthetic media in legal system

#### Pillar II — Build American AI Infrastructure

- **Streamlined permitting** for data centres, semiconductor facilities, and energy infrastructure
- **Grid development** to match AI pace — "reject radical climate dogma and bureaucratic red tape"
- **Semiconductor manufacturing** restoration (CHIPS Act continuation)
- **High-security data centres** for military and intelligence
- **Workforce** for AI infrastructure
- **Critical infrastructure cybersecurity**
- **Secure-by-design** AI technologies
- **Federal AI incident response** capacity

#### Pillar III — Lead in International AI Diplomacy and Security

- **Export American AI** to allies
- **Counter Chinese influence** in international governance bodies
- **Strengthen AI compute export controls**
- **Plug loopholes** in semiconductor export controls
- **Align protection measures globally**
- **Evaluate national security risks** in frontier models — "Ensure that the U.S. Government is at the Forefront of Evaluating National Security Risks in Frontier Models"
- **Invest in biosecurity**

## Observations

### The administration transition as governance failure mode

The Biden-to-Trump transition illustrates the fundamental weakness of executive-order-based AI governance. A comprehensive, internationally referenced framework (EO 14110) was entirely revoked on day one of the next administration. Mandatory reporting requirements, red-teaming mandates, content labelling directives, and agency rulemaking efforts were all cancelled. This makes executive orders unsuitable as durable governance infrastructure.

**Contrast with NIST AI RMF**: mandated by bipartisan legislation, the RMF survived the transition (though the Action Plan directs content revisions). Statutory mandates are more durable than executive orders.

**Contrast with EU**: the AI Act is legislation, not executive action. It survives administration changes in all 27 Member States.

### The competitiveness-vs-safety framing

Biden: "Safe, Secure, and Trustworthy" — safety as the primary organising principle.
Trump: "Removing Barriers" / "Winning the Race" — competitiveness as the primary organising principle.

Both acknowledge both dimensions — Biden's EO promoted innovation; Trump's Action Plan mentions safety R&D. But the hierarchy matters: which value yields when they conflict? Under Biden, innovation yielded to safety concerns (mandatory reporting). Under Trump, safety concerns yield to innovation (revoke mandates, remove regulations).

### State-level pressure and federal preemption

The Action Plan's funding conditionality language — considering "a state's AI regulatory climate when making funding decisions" — is a shot across the bow at states like Colorado (AI Act effective February 2026). It does not formally preempt state legislation (which would require Congressional action) but uses federal funding as leverage against state-level AI regulation. This creates a two-track system where federal deregulation meets state regulation.

### NIST AI RMF revision directive

The instruction to "eliminate references to misinformation, Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion, and climate change" from the NIST AI RMF is unprecedented. NIST has historically maintained technical independence; directing specific content removal based on political rather than technical criteria raises questions about the RMF's credibility as an independent technical standard.

### National security exception to deregulation

The Action Plan's Pillar III reveals that frontier model safety evaluation continues — but reframed as national security rather than public safety. "Ensure that the U.S. Government is at the Forefront of Evaluating National Security Risks in Frontier Models" preserves the evaluation function that AISI was performing, but narrowed to national security risks and decoupled from the broader public-safety mandate.

### Worker protections

Biden's EO explicitly referenced collective bargaining, worker surveillance limits, and labour union engagement. Trump's Action Plan states AI will "complement" workers' work — "not replacing it" — but specifies no mechanisms for worker consultation, transition support, or bargaining.

## Overall Assessment

The US federal AI governance landscape is defined by **volatility and structural weakness**:

- **Volatile**: the most comprehensive federal AI framework (EO 14110) was entirely revoked within 15 months
- **Structurally weak**: executive orders are the primary governance instrument; no comprehensive federal AI legislation exists
- **Durable elements**: NIST AI RMF (statutory mandate) and state-level legislation (Colorado AI Act) are more resilient to federal administration changes
- **Current direction**: competitiveness-first, deregulatory, geopolitically competitive with China, sceptical of state-level regulation

For UK-based firms:

1. **Do not build US AI compliance on executive orders.** They can be revoked overnight. Build on NIST AI RMF (statutory) and state-level requirements (where you have users).

2. **Monitor state-level legislation.** Colorado AI Act (effective February 2026) and potential California legislation may create binding obligations regardless of federal deregulation.

3. **The US federal deregulatory posture increases EU compliance advantage.** US firms face fewer domestic obligations, making EU AI Act compliance a competitive differentiator for trust-conscious clients.

4. **National security framing preserves frontier model evaluation.** AISI's function continues but reframed. UK-US bilateral safety cooperation (UK AISI ↔ US AISI) may continue under national security rather than public safety framing.

5. **The NIST AI RMF remains the operational reference** despite political pressures. Its four-function structure is durable and internationally aligned.
