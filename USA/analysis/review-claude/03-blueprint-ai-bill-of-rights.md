# Blueprint for an AI Bill of Rights — Review

**Document:** Blueprint for an AI Bill of Rights: Making Automated Systems Work for the American People
**Published:** October 2022
**Issuing body:** White House Office of Science and Technology Policy (OSTP), Biden administration
**Legal status:** Non-binding white paper; explicitly "does not constitute U.S. government policy"; creates no legal rights or obligations
**Length:** ~73 pages
**Current status:** Effectively superseded. Not formally revoked (it was never binding) but the Trump administration's AI policy framework (EO 14179, AI Action Plan) operates on different philosophical foundations.

---

## Summary

The Blueprint identifies five principles for the design, deployment, and governance of automated systems, oriented around protecting Americans' civil rights and democratic values. The five principles are: (1) safe and effective systems, (2) algorithmic discrimination protections, (3) data privacy, (4) notice and explanation, and (5) human alternatives, consideration, and fallback. Each principle is accompanied by expectations for how it should be applied, though none are binding. The document represents the high-water mark of Biden-era aspirational AI rights framing.

---

## Key Points

### Five Principles

1. **Safe and Effective Systems** — systems should undergo pre-deployment testing, risk identification, and ongoing monitoring; independent evaluation and reporting should be available
2. **Algorithmic Discrimination Protections** — systems should not contribute to unjustified differential treatment based on race, colour, ethnicity, sex, religion, age, national origin, disability, veteran status, or genetic information; proactive equity assessments, representative data, and independent evaluation
3. **Data Privacy** — built-in protections from abusive data practices; user agency over data use; design choices should not default to maximum data collection; surveillance technologies should be subject to heightened oversight
4. **Notice and Explanation** — clear notification that an automated system is being used; explanation of how and why it contributed to outcomes; timely, accessible, in plain language
5. **Human Alternatives, Consideration, and Fallback** — ability to opt out of automated systems; access to a human who can remedy problems; human consideration for sensitive domains (criminal justice, healthcare, education, employment)

### Scope

The Blueprint applies to "automated systems that have the potential to meaningfully impact the American public's rights, opportunities, or access to critical resources or services." It does not apply to all software — only systems with meaningful impact. It explicitly excludes national security and certain law enforcement activities.

---

## Observations

The Blueprint is historically important as the first US government articulation of citizen-centric AI rights. Its five principles directly parallel rights frameworks in other jurisdictions (EU AI Act's transparency and human oversight requirements; Singapore's Model Framework human-centricity principle; UK's five cross-sectoral principles including contestability and redress).

Its fundamental weakness was always its legal status: explicitly non-binding, creating no rights, no obligations, and no enforcement mechanism. It was aspirational by design — intended to start a conversation rather than conclude one. The Trump administration's different philosophical orientation means the conversation has moved in a different direction at the federal level, though the principles continue to influence state-level AI legislation (Colorado AI Act echoes several Blueprint principles).
