# EFTA and AI Governance

The four EFTA members — Norway, Iceland, Liechtenstein, and Switzerland — illustrate how the same trade bloc can produce fundamentally different AI governance obligations depending on one structural variable: **EEA membership**.

---

## The structural split

| Country | EFTA | EEA | EU AI Act binding? | Approach |
|---------|------|-----|-------------------|----------|
| **Norway** | Yes | Yes | Yes (via EEA incorporation) | Implementing via KI-loven (proposed law); Nkom designated as supervisory authority |
| **Iceland** | Yes | Yes | Yes (via EEA incorporation) | AI Action Plan 2025-2027; 25 measures across 5 areas; preparing for AI Act incorporation |
| **Liechtenstein** | Yes | Yes | Yes (via EEA incorporation) | Digital Innovation Unit preparing implementation; signed Council of Europe AI Convention Feb 2025 |
| **Switzerland** | Yes | **No** | **No** | Explicitly rejected AI Act; sector-specific + Council of Europe Convention approach |

The three EEA members (Norway, Iceland, Liechtenstein) must incorporate the EU AI Act into the EEA Agreement through the EEA Joint Committee. Once incorporated, the Act becomes binding domestic law — with the same prohibited practices, high-risk obligations, GPAI provisions, transparency requirements, and penalty structures as EU Member States face. Switzerland, as the only EFTA member outside the EEA, can choose not to adopt the AI Act.

**The democratic-input question.** The three EEA EFTA states participate as observers in the AI Board and in EEA Joint Committee deliberations, but they did not vote in the European Parliament or Council on the AI Act itself. They receive the obligations without having had full legislative voice. This is the standard EEA dynamic (also applicable to financial services, data protection, product safety), but the AI Act's breadth makes it particularly consequential — these three states are adopting one of the most ambitious regulatory frameworks in history through a consultative rather than legislative process.

## Norway

Norway has the most developed AI governance position of the four EFTA states.

### National AI Strategy (2020)

Norway published its National Strategy for Artificial Intelligence in January 2020, focusing on:
- AI in health, maritime, energy, and public administration
- Human-centric and trustworthy AI
- Data availability and sharing
- Research and skills
- Public sector as early adopter

The strategy was policy-led and non-binding — similar in approach to Singapore's NAIS 1.0.

### AI Act Implementation — KI-loven

On 30 June 2025, the Digitaliserings- og forvaltningsdepartementet (Ministry of Digitalisation and Public Governance) published a consultation package proposing a new Norwegian AI law (**KI-loven**) to implement the EU AI Act. Key elements:

- Incorporates the EU AI Act into Norwegian law
- Adds national provisions where the Regulation allows discretion
- **Norwegian Communications Authority (Nkom)** designated as national coordinating supervisory authority, working with sector regulators
- **Norsk akkreditering (NA)** designated as national accreditation authority for conformity assessment bodies
- Rules on jurisdictional scope, regulatory sandboxes, and national registers
- Targeted entry into force: **late summer 2026** — aligning with the EU's high-risk and transparency obligation dates

Minister Karianne Tung: "The Government is now making sure that Norway can exploit the opportunities afforded by [AI] development while maintaining public confidence."

Trade Minister Cecilie Myrseth: EU alignment ensures "Norwegian companies to compete" under identical European standards while promoting "innovation and accountability."

### KI Norge — National AI Arena

Norway established **AI Norway (KI Norge)** as a new entity within the Norwegian Digitalisation Agency (Digdir). It serves as:
- Expert hub and advisory service connecting public sector, industry, research, and academia
- Operator of an **AI Sandbox** — a controlled environment for businesses to experiment, develop, and train AI systems safely, with particular support for startups and SMEs
- Cooperation partner with Nkom and Datatilsynet (Data Protection Authority) for guidance and regulatory sandbox operations

### Datatilsynet AI Sandbox

The Norwegian Data Protection Authority (Datatilsynet) operates a separate **AI regulatory sandbox** focused on data-protection-related AI questions. This is one of the few operational AI regulatory sandboxes in Europe (the AI Act encourages Member States to establish them under Article 57).

### NORA — Norwegian AI Research Consortium

NORA (Norwegian Artificial Intelligence Research Consortium) brings together Norwegian universities and research institutions working on AI. Norway's research strengths include maritime AI, energy/oil & gas AI, and healthcare AI — reflecting the national economy's structure.

### Summary

Norway is the EFTA state most actively preparing for AI Act implementation. It has a dedicated law (KI-loven) in consultation, a designated supervisory authority (Nkom), a national AI arena (KI Norge), operational regulatory sandboxes, and a research consortium. Among EEA EFTA states, Norway's approach most closely resembles a mid-sized EU Member State's implementation.

## Iceland

Iceland's AI governance is smaller in scale but distinctive for its focus on language preservation and its ambitious action plan relative to the country's size (~380,000 people).

### AI Action Plan 2025-2027

Iceland published its first National AI Action Plan in 2025. Key elements:

**Five focus areas with 25 measures:**
1. **AI for societal benefit** — equitable access across all population segments
2. **Competitive working life** — business adoption support; workforce transition
3. **Education alignment** — AI literacy from primary through university
4. **Public sector implementation** — 30% efficiency gain target
5. **Healthcare integration** — diagnostics and system optimisation pilots

**Distinctive feature — Icelandic language preservation.** Iceland is one of the few countries to treat AI governance as a **linguistic and cultural preservation** issue. The plan commits to developing AI tools and resources for Icelandic to "maintain cultural identity in the digital age." This reflects a genuine existential concern for a language with ~370,000 native speakers: if AI systems do not support Icelandic, the language may face accelerated decline in digital contexts. Low-resource language support in AI is a policy issue that larger jurisdictions rarely address.

**Workforce projections**: 55% of jobs face AI-related changes; GDP growth projected at 0.8-6% annually through 2029 from AI adoption.

### AI Act Implementation

Iceland is preparing for EEA incorporation of the AI Act. No dedicated implementing law has been published yet (as of early 2026), but the Action Plan explicitly anticipates EU AI Act obligations including the full penalty structure (up to €35M or 7% of global turnover). Current data protection laws (Icelandic implementation of GDPR via EEA) apply in the interim.

### Summary

Iceland's approach is proportionate to its size — a focused action plan rather than extensive regulatory infrastructure. The language-preservation dimension is genuinely distinctive and highlights a governance concern that larger jurisdictions overlook.

## Liechtenstein

Liechtenstein is the smallest EFTA/EEA state (~39,000 people) with a proportionally small but notable AI governance position.

### Current status

- **Council of Europe AI Convention**: signed 27 February 2025; ratification under examination
- **EU AI Act**: will be binding once incorporated into EEA Agreement; **Digital Innovation Unit** leading implementation preparations
- **No standalone AI strategy or action plan** published

### Human rights monitoring

The Liechtenstein Human Rights Association (VMR) flagged in its 2024 monitoring report that the country needs to place "particular emphasis on the protection of human rights" when implementing AI laws, and create national regulation extending European laws to the private sector and national security — areas where international frameworks have gaps.

### The Switzerland-Liechtenstein divergence

Liechtenstein and Switzerland share a customs union, Swiss franc currency, and extensive bilateral integration. But on AI regulation, they are diverging:

- **Liechtenstein** (EEA member) will implement the EU AI Act — prohibited practices, high-risk obligations, GPAI provisions, transparency requirements, penalties
- **Switzerland** (non-EEA) explicitly rejected the AI Act — sector-specific approach, Council of Europe Convention, FDJP bill due end 2026

A firm operating from Vaduz faces AI Act obligations that the same firm operating from Zurich does not, for purely domestic activities. For EU-market-facing activities, both face the AI Act — but the domestic baseline differs. This is a micro-scale illustration of the regulatory fragmentation that the EU single market was designed to eliminate and that the EEA partially extends.

## Switzerland

Covered in detail in the [Switzerland analysis](../Switzerland/README.md). Summary position relative to the other three EFTA states:

- **Only EFTA member outside the EEA** — not bound by AI Act
- **Explicitly rejected comprehensive AI legislation** (Federal Council, February 2025)
- **Council of Europe AI Convention** as anchor (signed; ratification pending via FDJP bill due end 2026)
- **FINMA Guidance 08/2024** as the most operationally specific domestic AI governance instrument
- Swiss firms serving the EU comply with the AI Act directly — the domestic framework is additive

## Cross-EFTA observations

### The EEA dividing line is the most consequential variable

More consequential than GDP, AI ecosystem size, or governance philosophy. Norway, Iceland, and Liechtenstein will face the same AI Act obligations as France and Germany. Switzerland will not. This single structural variable produces more regulatory divergence within EFTA than any difference in national AI strategy.

### Supervisory capacity varies enormously

Norway has designated Nkom with sector-regulator coordination, plus Datatilsynet's sandbox, plus KI Norge. Iceland and Liechtenstein must establish supervisory structures but with far smaller institutional capacity. The AI Act's enforcement demands — conformity assessment, market surveillance, incident reporting coordination — are designed for states with multiple regulatory agencies and significant compliance infrastructure. Whether Liechtenstein (39,000 people) can sustain a meaningful AI Act supervisory function is an open question.

### Democratic input is limited for all three EEA states

The AI Act was adopted by the European Parliament and Council. Norway, Iceland, and Liechtenstein participate as observers and through EEA Joint Committee consultation but did not vote. This is standard EEA practice but worth noting: three states are implementing one of the most significant regulatory frameworks of the decade through a process that gives them voice but not vote.

### Regulatory sandboxes as proportionate response

Both Norway and Iceland have emphasised regulatory sandboxes — controlled environments for AI testing and development. This reflects a pragmatic recognition that smaller economies cannot match the EU's regulatory capacity but can provide supportive innovation environments. The AI Act itself encourages Member States to establish regulatory sandboxes (Article 57), making sandbox operation a compliance-aligned activity.

### Language and cultural preservation

Iceland's focus on Icelandic-language AI resources highlights a governance dimension that large-language-community jurisdictions (UK, EU, US) rarely address. Switzerland (with four national languages, including Romansh at ~40,000 speakers) faces a similar but less acute version. Norway (Bokmål, Nynorsk, Sámi languages) also has minority-language considerations. Small-state AI governance inherently involves questions about whether AI systems will support the state's linguistic and cultural infrastructure — a concern absent from most AI governance frameworks.

### EFTA-internal divergence will increase

As the AI Act enters full application (August 2026 for high-risk and transparency obligations), the gap between the three EEA EFTA states (implementing the Act) and Switzerland (not implementing it) will widen. Swiss firms operating in Norway, Iceland, or Liechtenstein will face AI Act obligations in those jurisdictions. The customs-union relationship between Switzerland and Liechtenstein creates particularly interesting edge cases where the same product may face different AI governance requirements depending on which side of the border it is offered.

## Relevance for UK firms

For UK-based firms, the EFTA landscape matters in three ways:

1. **Norway is a significant trading partner.** UK firms serving Norwegian clients will face AI Act obligations via Norwegian implementation (KI-loven), not directly from the EU. The obligations are substantively identical but the supervisory authority (Nkom) and enforcement jurisdiction are Norwegian.

2. **The EEA extends the AI Act's effective reach.** The AI Act applies not just across the EU 27 but also across Norway, Iceland, and Liechtenstein — making the effective regulatory zone ~450 million people, not 450 million. UK firms already tracking EU AI Act compliance should note that the same framework applies in these three additional markets.

3. **Switzerland remains the exception.** UK-Swiss business relationships benefit from the lighter Swiss domestic framework, but both UK and Swiss firms must still comply with the AI Act when serving EU/EEA markets.
