# Small Open Economies and AI Governance: UK, Singapore, Switzerland

A comparison of three jurisdictions that rejected comprehensive AI legislation in favour of voluntary, sector-specific, or principles-based approaches — and all face the EU AI Act as the practical compliance floor for firms serving European markets.

Based on 14 UK reviews, 5 Singapore analyses, 2 Switzerland reviews, and the [UK-EU comparison](UK-EU-comparison.md).

---

## The shared pattern

The UK, Singapore, and Switzerland have each independently arrived at a similar governance posture:

1. **No comprehensive AI Act.** All three explicitly rejected horizontal binding AI legislation.
2. **Voluntary and/or sector-regulator-led.** Governance operates through frameworks, guidance, and existing regulators rather than new statutory obligations.
3. **Pro-innovation framing.** All three position their regulatory approach as a competitive advantage against the EU's heavier-touch model.
4. **EU AI Act as external constraint.** All three have significant economic relationships with the EU. Firms in all three jurisdictions that serve EU users must comply with the AI Act regardless of domestic law.
5. **International positioning as distinctive contribution.** Each has carved out a niche in the global AI governance landscape that leverages its strengths rather than competing head-on with the EU's regulatory mass.

These convergences are not coordinated — they reflect structural similarities in the position of mid-sized, trade-dependent economies that are large enough to have sophisticated AI ecosystems but too small to impose their regulatory preferences extraterritorially the way the EU (single market scale) or US (Big Tech home jurisdiction) can.

## Where they diverge

### Depth of governance infrastructure

**UK** has the deepest governance infrastructure by volume — 14 substantive documents covering strategy, regulation, operational guidance, sector-specific policy, safety evaluations, compute, legislation, and international scientific consensus. The AI Playbook alone (118 pages) is more prescriptive than Singapore's entire governance stack.

**Singapore** has the most systematically layered governance infrastructure — three generations of frameworks (2020 → 2024 → 2026) that build on each other without breaking changes, plus practical testing tools (AI Verify, Project Moonshot). What Singapore lacks in volume it compensates for in coherence.

**Switzerland** has the thinnest governance infrastructure — a one-page Federal Council decision and a 7-page FINMA guidance. This reflects a genuine political choice (no AI Act needed) rather than a gap to be filled. The FDJP bill (due end 2026) will add substance, but Swiss AI governance will remain minimal compared to the other two.

### Safety vs governance emphasis

**UK** emphasises frontier AI safety. AISI conducts pre-deployment evaluations of the most capable models. The International Scientific Report addresses catastrophic and existential risks. The UK's AI governance discourse is safety-led.

**Singapore** emphasises governance and testing tools. AI Verify provides structured governance validation against 11 principles. The GenAI Framework's nine dimensions cover a broader canvas than safety alone (accountability, data, copyright, content provenance, public good). Singapore's discourse is governance-and-deployment-led.

**Switzerland** emphasises neither — its AI governance is positioned as part of broader digital strategy and data protection rather than as a distinctive policy domain. FINMA's guidance is risk-management-led within financial services but does not extend beyond that sector.

### Treatment of agentic AI

**Singapore** is the global leader: the Agentic AI Governance Framework (January 2026) is the first national-level governance document for agentic AI systems anywhere. It identifies the architectural distinctiveness of agents (action-taking, multi-step planning, tool use, trust accumulation) as the central governance challenge.

**UK** and **Switzerland** have no agentic AI governance documents. The UK's AISI evaluations address frontier model capabilities (which include agentic properties) but there is no dedicated governance framework for agentic deployment.

### EU equivalence pressure

**Switzerland** faces the most direct EU equivalence pressure. Switzerland's bilateral framework with the EU (covering mutual recognition of conformity assessments, particularly in MedTech and pharma) means that regulatory divergence has immediate market-access consequences. The FDJP bill must consider this explicitly.

**UK** faces significant but less direct pressure. Post-Brexit, the UK has no bilateral framework requiring regulatory equivalence. The pressure is commercial — firms serving both markets adopt EU standards globally — rather than treaty-based.

**Singapore** faces the least EU-specific pressure. Singapore's trade with the EU is significant but not as structurally dependent as Switzerland's. Singapore's firms exporting to the EU comply with the AI Act as a commercial matter rather than a market-access-framework requirement. Singapore faces more pressure from ASEAN harmonisation than from EU equivalence.

### Compute sovereignty

**UK** has the most ambitious compute sovereignty agenda: specific infrastructure (Isambard-AI, DAWN, EPCC), a dedicated review (Ghahramani, Future of Compute Review), a 20x AIRR expansion target, and 5 AI Growth Zones with dedicated grid and planning reform.

**Singapore** takes a pragmatic approach: crowd in compute from CSP partnerships, allocate carbon/power budget, manage a subset of GPUs for public-good use. Singapore accepts it will not be a global compute leader but ensures adequate capacity for national priorities.

**Switzerland** has no specific AI compute strategy. It leverages existing academic infrastructure (ETH, EPFL, CERN) and commercial provision. Compute is not treated as a strategic AI policy lever.

### Financial-sector AI governance

All three have financial-sector AI guidance, making this the best apples-to-apples comparison:

**UK FCA AI Update** (April 2024): maps the government's 5 principles onto existing frameworks (Principles for Business, SYSC, SM&CR, Consumer Duty). No AI-specific rules. Technology-agnostic supervision.

**Singapore MAS FEAT Principles** (November 2018): four principles (Fairness, Ethics, Accountability, Transparency) for AI/data analytics in financial services. Accompanied by FEAT assessment methodology and Veritas toolkit for validation.

**Switzerland FINMA Guidance 08/2024**: seven areas of supervisory assessment (governance, inventory, data quality, testing, documentation, explainability, independent review). Principle-based, proportionate, technology-neutral.

These three documents share DNA — principle-based, proportionate, technology-neutral, relying on existing supervisory frameworks. The differences are in specificity: FINMA is the most concrete (7 named assessment areas with findings from supervisory practice); MAS is the most structured (4 named principles with dedicated testing toolkit); FCA is the most deferential (maps principles without adding AI-specific requirements).

## Where they converge

### The EU as compliance floor

The single most important convergence: **all three jurisdictions' firms, when serving EU users, must comply with the EU AI Act regardless of domestic regulation.**

This creates a structural asymmetry: domestic governance frameworks are additive — they sit on top of the EU floor, not instead of it. A UK-based GPAI provider, a Singapore-headquartered MedTech firm, and a Swiss bank all face the same EU obligations when their products enter the EU market.

The practical consequence is that domestic governance frameworks primarily govern:
- **Domestic-only AI use** (not exported to EU)
- **Public sector AI** (government use within the jurisdiction)
- **Areas the EU doesn't cover** (agentic AI governance in Singapore's case; operational public-sector guidance in the UK's case)

For export-oriented AI firms, domestic governance is reputation and self-governance; the EU AI Act is compliance.

### Voluntary frameworks as soft infrastructure

All three jurisdictions have concluded that voluntary governance frameworks are more useful than no frameworks at all, even without enforcement teeth. The rationale differs slightly:

- **UK**: voluntary principles allow sector regulators to adapt to context; statute would freeze today's assumptions
- **Singapore**: voluntary frameworks build ecosystem trust and international credibility; they are the "governance tool" offering
- **Switzerland**: existing law is sufficient; additional governance should be proportionate and not burden innovation

But the practical outcome is similar: firms in all three jurisdictions that take governance seriously use these frameworks as internal governance scaffolding, while compliance is driven by the EU AI Act (for EU-facing activities) and adjacent domestic law (data protection, consumer protection, financial regulation).

### International positioning as comparative advantage

Each has found a niche:
- **UK**: scientific safety leadership (AISI, International Scientific Report, summit series)
- **Singapore**: practical governance tools (AI Verify, frameworks) + ASEAN regional influence
- **Switzerland**: diplomatic convening (Geneva, Council of Europe, ITU)

These are complementary rather than competitive. A global AI governance architecture needs all three functions: scientific evaluation, practical governance tools, and diplomatic forums. The three jurisdictions collectively provide what the EU's regulatory-setting approach does not.

### The innovation framing

All three explicitly frame their governance approach as innovation-friendly. This framing serves a dual purpose:

1. **Domestic**: reassures industry that regulation will not be burdensome
2. **International**: positions the jurisdiction as an alternative to the EU's heavier-touch model for firms choosing where to locate AI operations

Whether this framing reflects genuine policy substance or marketing is debatable. The UK has the strongest claim (AISI, compute investment, AI Growth Zones are real infrastructure, not just regulatory absence). Singapore has a strong claim (AI Verify, governance frameworks, start-up ecosystem). Switzerland's claim is thinner — "innovation-friendly" in Switzerland currently means "we haven't regulated yet" rather than "we've built specific pro-innovation infrastructure."

## The structural question

The deeper question behind all three jurisdictions' approaches is: **can a small open economy maintain a distinctive AI governance posture when the EU AI Act sets the global compliance floor?**

The evidence so far suggests: **partially, and in specific areas.**

**Where domestic governance distinctiveness has value:**
- Public sector AI use (UK Playbook, Singapore government AI adoption)
- Frontier safety evaluation (UK AISI — genuinely distinctive and world-leading)
- Practical governance tools (Singapore AI Verify — genuinely distinctive)
- Domestic-only AI applications not exported to EU
- International convening and influence (all three)

**Where domestic governance distinctiveness is nominal:**
- Export-oriented AI products (EU AI Act applies regardless)
- GPAI providers (EU Chapter V applies if model serves EU users)
- Financial services firms operating cross-border (EU + domestic regulator obligations stack)
- MedTech, pharma, automotive (EU conformity assessment applies through product regulation)

**The honest assessment:** for a UK, Singaporean, or Swiss firm building an AI product for global markets, the EU AI Act is the compliance baseline and domestic governance frameworks are reputation-enhancers. The reverse is not true — EU compliance alone does not satisfy all domestic expectations (e.g., Singapore's governance frameworks, UK's AISI engagement).

For firms operating **only domestically** with no EU market exposure, the lighter-touch domestic frameworks are genuinely enabling. But the proportion of AI firms that can afford to ignore the EU market entirely is small and shrinking.

## Practical implications

For firms and advisors working across these three jurisdictions:

1. **Build compliance to the EU AI Act floor.** All three jurisdictions' firms serving EU users must meet the EU standard. This is the non-negotiable baseline.

2. **Layer domestic governance on top.** UK firms should engage with AISI and use the Playbook. Singapore firms should align with the Model Framework stack and AI Verify. Swiss firms should comply with FINMA guidance. These are additive, not substitutive.

3. **Leverage the distinctive strengths.** UK AISI evaluations, Singapore AI Verify testing, and Switzerland's neutral convening role are genuine assets that firms can use regardless of jurisdiction.

4. **Watch for divergence.** UK's DUAA 2025 already diverges from EU on ADM. Singapore's agentic AI framework covers territory the EU hasn't reached. Switzerland's FDJP bill (due end 2026) may create new obligations. Divergence between these frameworks and the EU will increase, not decrease.

5. **Treat "pro-innovation" claims with appropriate scepticism.** All three jurisdictions market their AI governance as innovation-friendly. But innovation-friendliness is a function of what you build (compute, tools, infrastructure) not just what you don't regulate. Assess each jurisdiction by what it provides, not just what it avoids.
